  <meta name="citation_abstract" content="Most angiosperms bear hermaphroditic flowers, but a few species have evolved outcrossing strategies, such as dioecy, the presence of separate male and female individuals. We previously investigated the mechanisms underlying dioecy in diploid persimmon (D. lotus) and found that male flowers are specified by repression of the autosomal gene MeGI by its paralog, the Y-encoded pseudo-gene OGI. This mechanism is thought to be lineage-specific, but its evolutionary path remains unknown. Here, we developed a full draft of the diploid persimmon genome (D. lotus), which revealed a lineage-specific whole-genome duplication event and provided information on the architecture of the Y chromosome. We also identified three paralogs, MeGI, OGI and newly identified Sister of MeGI (SiMeGI). Evolutionary analysis suggested that MeGI underwent adaptive evolution after the whole-genome duplication event. Transformation of tobacco plants with MeGI and SiMeGI revealed that MeGI specifically acquired a new function as a repressor of male organ development, while SiMeGI presumably maintained the original function. Later, a segmental duplication event spawned MeGI&rsquo;s regulator OGI on the Y-chromosome, completing the path leading to dioecy, and probably initiating the formation of the Y-chromosome. These findings exemplify how duplication events can provide flexible genetic material available to help respond to varying environments and provide interesting parallels for our understanding of the mechanisms underlying the transition into dieocy in plants.">

<div class="abstract toc-section abstract-type-"><a id="abstract0" name="abstract0" data-toc="abstract0" class="link-target" title="Abstract"></a><h2>Abstract</h2><div class="abstract-content"><a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Most angiosperms bear hermaphroditic flowers, but a few species have evolved outcrossing strategies, such as dioecy, the presence of separate male and female individuals. We previously investigated the mechanisms underlying dioecy in diploid persimmon (<em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em>) and found that male flowers are specified by repression of the autosomal gene <em>MeGI</em> by its paralog, the Y-encoded pseudo-gene <em>OGI</em>. This mechanism is thought to be lineage-specific, but its evolutionary path remains unknown. Here, we developed a full draft of the diploid persimmon genome (<em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em>), which revealed a lineage-specific whole-genome duplication event and provided information on the architecture of the Y chromosome. We also identified three paralogs, <em>MeGI</em>, <em>OGI</em> and newly identified <em>Sister of MeGI</em> (<em>SiMeGI</em>). Evolutionary analysis suggested that <em>MeGI</em> underwent adaptive evolution after the whole-genome duplication event. Transformation of tobacco plants with <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> revealed that <em>MeGI</em> specifically acquired a new function as a repressor of male organ development, while <em>SiMeGI</em> presumably maintained the original function. Later, a segmental duplication event spawned <em>MeGI</em>’s regulator <em>OGI</em> on the Y-chromosome, completing the path leading to dioecy, and probably initiating the formation of the Y-chromosome. These findings exemplify how duplication events can provide flexible genetic material available to help respond to varying environments and provide interesting parallels for our understanding of the mechanisms underlying the transition into dieocy in plants.</p>
</div></div><div class="abstract toc-section abstract-type-summary"><a id="abstract1" name="abstract1" data-toc="abstract1" class="link-target" title="Author summary"></a>
<h2>Author summary</h2>
<div class="abstract-content"><a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract2.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Plant sexuality has fascinated scientists for decades. Most plants can self-reproduce but not all. For example, a small subset of species have evolved a system called dioecy, with separate male and female individuals. Dioecy has evolved multiple times independently and, while we do not understand the molecular mechanisms underlying dioecy in many of these species yet, a picture is starting to emerge with recent progress in several dioecious species. Here, we focused on the evolutionary events leading to dioecy in persimmon. Our previous work had identified a pair of genes regulating sex in this species, called <em>OGI</em> and <em>MeGI</em>. We drafted the whole genome sequence of diploid persimmon to investigate their evolutionary history. We discovered a lineage-specific whole-genome duplication event, and observed that <em>MeGI</em> underwent adaptive evolution after this event. Transgenic analyses validated that <em>MeGI</em> newly acquired a male-suppressor function, while the other copy of this gene, <em>SiMeGI</em>, did not. The regulator of <em>MeGI</em>, <em>OGI</em>, resulted from a second smaller-scale segmental duplication event, finalizing the system. This study sheds light on the role of duplication as a mechanism that promote flexible genes functions, and how it can affect important biological functions, such as the establishment of a new sexual system.</p>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec001" name="sec001" data-toc="sec001" class="link-target" title="Introduction"></a><h2>Introduction</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Most species of flowering plants are hermaphrodite, but a small proportion have genetically determined separate sexes [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref001" class="ref-tip">1</a>]. The rarity of dioecy contrasts with its broad distribution across the flowering plant phylogenetic tree, suggesting multiple independent transitions into dioecy. Our study aimed to understand the molecular and evolutionary mechanisms underlying such changes. Advances in genomic analyses have allowed studies of plant sex chromosomes in a few dioecious plant species including papaya and <em>Silene</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref002" class="ref-tip">2</a>–<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref004" class="ref-tip">4</a>], and a few genetic sex determining genes have recently been identified, including in the persimmon, kiwifruit, and asparagus [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>–<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>]. Consistent with theoretical models [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref008" class="ref-tip">8</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>], the results indicate that at least one gain-of-function mutation occurred in the evolution of dioecy, creating a dominant gynoecium or androecium suppressor. Data from these species is also consistent with gene duplication events as the first event leading to these gain-of-function mutations, because the redundancy provided by the presence of duplicate copies allows one copy to be neofunctionalized without loss of the original function [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref010" class="ref-tip">10</a>]. Unlike many animal taxa, flowering plants have experienced numerous whole-genome duplication events (WGD) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref011" class="ref-tip">11</a>], which are thought to have provided opportunities for the appearance of new traits specific to each plant species. For example, functional differentiation between paralogs, which had been derived from whole-genome duplication (WGD), resulted in the establishment of ripening characteristics in tomato fruits [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref012" class="ref-tip">12</a>], and potentially enabled the adaptation to life underwater in seagrass (<em>Zostera marina</em>) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref013" class="ref-tip">13</a>].</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>Within the large order Ericales, a heterogametic male (XY) sex determination system has evolved independently in at least two genera, <em>Diospyros</em> and <em>Actinidia</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>]. <em>Diospyros</em> had evolved a Y-encoded pseudogene called <em>OGI</em>, that produces small-RNA, which in turn repress the autosomal feminization gene, <em>MeGI</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. <em>MeGI</em> belongs to the HD-Zip1 gene family conserved across angiosperms, but the specific function of <em>MeGI</em> to act for repression of male function, or feminization, has not been observed in <em>MeGI</em> orthologs from other plants so far [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>–<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>]. Indeed, although <em>Actinidia</em> and <em>Diospyros</em> are phylogenetically close to each other, the Y-encoded sex determination system in <em>Actinidia</em> does not involve the <em>MeGI</em> ortholog or another member of the HD-Zip1 family [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>]. The existence of <em>MeGI</em>, <em>OGI</em>, and a third paralog called <em>Sister-of-MeGI</em> (<em>SiMeGI</em>), which was newly identified in this study, provide the opportunity to investigate both the scale and context of the duplication events that triggered the appearance of a lineage-specific sex determination system in this species. To address this question, we sequenced the genome of Caucasian diploid persimmon, focusing on the lineage-specific duplication events. Evolutionary analyses on the duplicated pairs found a limited numbers of the genes which were potentially neofunctionalized via adaptive evolution after the duplication. Our results provide a potential path from the duplicated paralogs of a HD-Zip1 to dioecy, and shed light on how lineage-specific duplication events contribute to the evolution of a new sex determination system in a plant species.</p>
</div>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec003" name="sec003" class="link-target" title="Draft genome sequencing of Diospyros"></a>
<h3>Draft genome sequencing of Diospyros</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Initially, we assembled a draft genome from ca 65X PacBio long read coverage of the expected haploid genome size (907Mb from flow cytometry [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>], 877.7Mb from kmer analysis) using Falcon (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s001">S1 Fig</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s016">S1 Table</a>). This resulted in 3,073 primary contigs totaling 746.1Mb, which covers ca 85% of the genome, and 5,901 “secondary” contigs, which are putative allelic contigs to the primary contigs. Next, we built three genetic maps, created from two segregating F1 populations (N = 314 and 119, see <a href="#sec009">Materials and Methods</a> and <a href="#pgen.1008566.s017">S2 Table</a>). <div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec004" name="sec004" class="link-target" title="Identification of a whole-genome duplication event specific to the Diospyros genus"></a>
<h3>Identification of a whole-genome duplication event specific to the Diospyros genus</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>To investigate gene duplication patterns, we analyzed the distribution of silent divergence rate (<em>dS</em>) between homologous gene pairs. We compared the distribution of silent divergence rate of homologous gene pairs within the persimmon genome, with those within the kiwifruit (<em>Actinidia</em>), tomato (<em>Solanum</em>) and grape (<em>Vitis</em>) genomes. A subset of persimmon genes formed a clear peak of silent divergence rate (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g002">Fig 2A</a>, <em>dS</em> = ca 0.5–0.9, mode <em>dS</em> = 0.69), suggesting that a whole-genome duplication (WGD) event, named <em>Dd</em>-α, occurred in this clade. Next, we performed a genome-wide synteny analysis, based on the location of the gene pairs with <em>dS</em> values ranging from 0.5 and 0.9, using SynMap in CoGe [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref020" class="ref-tip">20</a>] (<a href="https://genomevolution.org/coge/">https://genomevolution.org/coge/</a>). The results indicated long syntenic blocks throughout the persimmon genome (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s004">S4A and S4B Fig</a>). The genomic regions including the gene pairs in this peak exhibited long regions of synteny (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s005">S5 Fig</a>). The distribution of four-fold synonymous (degenerative) third-codon transversion (4DTv) supported this lineage-specific WGD (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g002">Fig 2B</a>). Comparison of intraspecific <em>dS</em> between homologous gene pairs in the <em>Diospyros</em> genome and interspecific <em>dS</em> between the orthologs from <em>Diospyros</em> and <em>Actinidia</em>, or from <em>Diospyros</em> and <em>Vitis</em>, indicated that the <em>Dd</em>-α event postdated the divergence of <em>Diospyros</em> and <em>Actinidia</em>, and might coincide with the divergence of the Ebenaceae family (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g002">Fig 2C and 2D</a>). Two other events, <em>Ad-α</em> and <em>Ad-β</em>, have been inferred by a similar analysis in the <em>Actinidia</em> genome [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>] (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s006">S6 Fig</a>) but are not detectable in the <em>Diospyros</em> genome. Thus, <em>Actinidia</em> and <em>Diospyros</em> differ by at least three lineage-specific ancestral WGD events. These occurred at a time similar to previously reported whole-genome duplication events in the asterids [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref021" class="ref-tip">21</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref022" class="ref-tip">22</a>], as well as across the angiosperms [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref011" class="ref-tip">11</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref023" class="ref-tip">23</a>], concentrated around the K-Pg (Cretaceous-Paleogen) boundary (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g002">Fig 2E</a>).</p>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec005" name="sec005" class="link-target" title="Only a few gene, including MeGI, exhibit signs of positive selection but divergent expression patterns are common following the WGD event"></a>
<h3>Only a few gene, including MeGI, exhibit signs of positive selection but divergent expression patterns are common following the WGD event</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>To explore the evolutionary significance of lineage-specific duplications, and particularly of the <em>Dd-α</em> WGD event, <em>dN/dS</em> values between the duplicated gene pairs putatively derived from the <em>Dd-α</em> WGD events (N = 2,619) were calculated. The <em>dN/dS</em> values averaged over the coding regions indicated that most of the duplicates experienced either purifying or neutral selection (<em>dN/dS</em> ≤ 1.0, <a href="#pgen-1008566-g003">Fig 3A</a>). In contrast, site- and evolutionary branch-specific tests for positive selection (dN/dS &gt;&gt; 1.0), using PAML, suggested that at least 9 genes experienced strong positive selection (posterior probability &gt; 0.99 in Bayes Empirical Bayes analysis) following the <em>Dd-α</em> WGD event (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g003">Fig 3B and 3C</a>). Importantly, <em>MeGI</em> and its paralog, named <em>Sister of MeGI</em> (<em>SiMeGI</em>), were one of these 9 gene pairs. Consistently, <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> were included in the same gene family after OrthoMCL analysis. They are located on Chr 13 (Dlo_pri0799F.1) and Chr 4 (Dlo_pri0025F.1), respectively, and these regions showed syntenic collinearity around these genes based on sequence similarity (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g003">Fig 3D–3F</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s007">S7 Fig</a>). Syntenic blocks derived from gene order (with genes with <em>dS</em> values between 0.5 and 0.9) were observed with SynMap in CoGe as well (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g003">Fig 3E</a>). These findings are consistent with the hypothesis that they were derived from the <em>Dd-α</em> WGD event, although we cannot exclude the possibility that they were generated from a simple segmental duplication concurrent with the <em>Dd-α</em>.</p>

Such frequent variation in expression patterns is consistent with previous results in soybean [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref024" class="ref-tip">24</a>] and could have originated from rapid evolution in <em>cis</em>-motifs after WGD.</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec006" name="sec006" class="link-target" title="Adaptive evolution of MeGI to act specifically for repression of androecium development"></a>
<h3>Adaptive evolution of MeGI to act specifically for repression of androecium development</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Genome-wide survey of the HD-Zip1 family, to which <em>MeGI</em> belongs, found 34 genes in the <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> genome. Phylogenetic analysis of <em>MeGI/Vrs1</em> orthologs from representative angiosperm species indicated that only <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> belong in the <em>MeGI/Vrs1</em> clade (bootstrap = 100/100, <a href="#pgen-1008566-g004">Fig 4A</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s008">S8 Fig</a>). Finer evolutionary analysis on the <em>MeGI/SiMeGI</em> orthologs, to detect site-branch specific evolutionary rates using PAML, indicated that specific regions of <em>MeGI</em> experienced strong positive selection soon after the <em>Dd-α</em> event (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g004">Fig 4B</a>, p = 0.0027 for <em>dN/dS</em> &gt; 1.0, post. prob. &gt; 0.99 for P23-V40-S152, and <a href="#pgen-1008566-g004">Fig 4C and 4D</a>, <em>dN/dS</em> &gt; 2.0 for the region between 45 and 165 bp in the sliding window test).</p>

Constitutive induction of <em>MeGI</em> under the control of the CaMV35S promoter resulted in severely dwarfed plants and repressed androecium development (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">Fig 5A–5C</a>, <a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">5G and 5H</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s009">S9 Fig</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s020">S5 Table</a>), consistent with previous results using the same construct in <em>A</em>. <em>thaliana</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. On the other hand, constitutive induction of <em>SiMeGI</em> under the control of the same promoter resulted in plants of only slightly reduced stature and normal androecium development in <em>N</em>. <em>tabacum</em> (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">Fig 5D–5H</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s009">S9 Fig</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s021">S6 Table</a>). The function of <em>MeGI</em> as repressor of androecium in persimmon is due to the ability to regulate <em>PISTILLATA</em> (<em>PI</em>) in young developing androecium [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref025" class="ref-tip">25</a>]. The expression level of <em>PI</em> in <em>N</em>. <em>tabacum</em> was significantly down-regulated in the transgenic lines with <em>MeGI</em>, while the lines transformed with <em>SiMeGI</em> showed no changes in <em>PI</em> expression (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">Fig 5I and 5J</a>). In Arabidopsis, which is a very far lineage from <em>Diospyros</em>, high expression of <em>SiMeGI</em> typically did not result in altered flower morphology although it occasionally resulted in inhibited androecium development (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s010">S10 Fig</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.s022">S7</a> and <a href="#pgen.1008566.s023">S8</a> Tables).</p>

Specifically, <em>MeGI</em> exhibits higher expression than <em>SiMeGI</em> during the flower maturing stages (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">Fig 5L–5N</a>). This expression differentiation might also contribute to <em>MeGI-</em>specific feminizing function.</p>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec007" name="sec007" class="link-target" title="Formation of a lineage-specific, and slowly evolving Y-chromosome in Diospyros"></a>
<h3>Formation of a lineage-specific, and slowly evolving Y-chromosome in Diospyros</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>To investigate the sequence and structure of the sex chromosomes, we undertook the following steps. First, we anchored some pseudo-autosomal scaffolds to chromosome 15, using sex-linked SNPs markers, previously derived from the F1 population described above (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s024">S9 Table</a>). On the other hand, the male-specific region of the Y-chromosome, including <em>OGI</em>, could not be anchored using SNPs, presumably due to large structural variation between the X and Y chromosome at these locations. Therefore, we anchored 7 Y-chromosomal scaffolds surrounding <em>OGI</em> using the end-sequences of BAC clones selected for sequencing based on successive walking starting from <em>OGI</em> itself [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. We then assessed their genomic context by mapping short sequencing reads from male and female individuals of the KK population (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g005">Fig 5A</a>) to these scaffolds. The regions flanking <em>OGI</em> were male-specific or hyper-repetitive, often including palindrome-like structures (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s013">S13 Fig</a>), that are consistent with the sequence context of sex chromosomes in animal [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref030" class="ref-tip">30</a>]. Putative pseudo-autosomal region (PAR)-like sequences, which include both X- and Y-allelic genes, were observed only 200–300 kb from <em>OGI</em> (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g006">Fig 6A</a>). Such a short Y-specific region is consistent with our previous results [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>].</p>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec008" name="sec008" class="link-target" title="Transitions towards dioecy are associated with duplication events"></a>
<h3>Transitions towards dioecy are associated with duplication events</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Our results suggest the following working hypothesis for the evolutionary path into dioecy in <em>Diospyros</em>. The <em>Diospyros</em>-specific WGD event, <em>Dd-α</em> resulted in the appearance of <em>MeGI</em> and promoted the neofunctionalization of this gene into a dominant suppressor of androecium, as a feminization factor. This was followed by a second, segmental duplication of <em>MeGI</em> to derive a Y-encoded <em>OGI</em>, which is a dominant repressor of <em>MeGI</em> (<a href="#pgen-1008566-g007">Fig 7</a>). Interestingly, the information available so far from other dioecious species hints at the possibility that this type of pattern may have played a role in the evolution of dioecy in other species. For example, in the establishment of dioecy in garden asparagus, the Y-encoded putative sex determinant, <em>SOFF</em>, is thought to have originated from an <em>Asparagus</em>-specific gene duplication event, which was followed by the acquisition of its function as a dominant suppressor of feminization (SuF) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref006" class="ref-tip">6</a>]. Furthermore, the Y-encoded putative sex determinant in kiwifruit (<em>Actinidia</em> spp.), <em>Shy Girl</em>, which acts as a dominant suppressor of feminization, also arose via an <em>Actinidia</em>-specific duplication event [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>], probably one of the <em>Actinidia</em>-specific WGD events, <em>Ad-</em>α [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>]. These parallel paths towards the independent evolution of all three of these sex determinants is probably not coincidental, but consistent with the theoretical framework described above. In flowering plants, transition into separated sexuality requires the appearance and selection of a gain-of-function event in order to acquire a dominant suppressor(s), such as <em>MeGI</em>. Whole-genome duplication events provide good opportunities for such a scenario. The concentration of independent paleoplodization events in the K-Pg boundary is consistent with the adaptive evolution of plants against the substantial environmental changes, including mass extinction of their pollinators that took place at the time [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref011" class="ref-tip">11</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref031" class="ref-tip">31</a>]. A selfing habit engendered by polyploidy would be advantageous, but protracted evolutionary success would be favored by an eventual return to outcrossing. The neofunctionalization of <em>MeGI</em> resulting in the acquisition of a lineage-specific new sexual system could be one of these adaptive strategies. This hypothesis is also consistent with the observed wide diversity of sex determination system within plants.</p>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec009" name="sec009" data-toc="sec009" class="link-target" title="Materials and methods"></a><h2>Materials and methods</h2>
<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec010" name="sec010" class="link-target" title="Initial genome sequence assembly"></a>
<h3>Initial genome sequence assembly</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Dormant buds of <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> cv. Kunsenshi-male were burst in the dark for 2-weeks to harvest chlorophyll-starved young leaves (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s025">S10 Table</a>). High molecular weight DNA were extracted using the Genome-tip 100/G kit (QIAGEN, Tokyo, Japan), followed by purification using phenol/chloroform extraction. Libraries were size-selected using the Blue Pippin and the following size minimums: 12 kb (14 SMRT cells), 15 kb (34 SMRT cells) and 16 kb (12 SMRT cells). A total of 60 SMRT cells and 54 Gb of PacBio raw data were obtained using the PacBio RSII. Filtered sub-reads were pooled and the longest were retained for assembly, by removing all filtered subreads shorter than 12 kb. This resulted in approximately 32x coverage of the estimated 1 Gb haploid genome size. PacBio reads were assembled using Falcon, producing 3,417 primary contigs and 6,318 alternate contigs. Next, all contigs were assessed for the presence of contaminating sequences by aligning each contig to a custom database using BLASTN+ version 2.2.31+. The custom database contained Kiwifruit psuedomolecule (<a href="http://bioinfo.bti.cornell.edu/pub/kiwifruit/Kiwifruit_pseudomolecule.fa.gz">http://bioinfo.bti.cornell.edu/pub/kiwifruit/Kiwifruit_pseudomolecule.fa.gz</a>), the <em>A</em>. <em>thaliana</em> chromosomes (<a href="http://ftp.arabidopsis.org/home/tair/Genes/TAIR10_genome_release/TAIR10_chromosome_files/">http://ftp.arabidopsis.org/home/tair/Genes/TAIR10_genome_release/TAIR10_chromosome_files/</a>), as well as the human draft genome and representative bacterial / archaeal genome databases (pre-formatted blast+ database <a href="http://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast/documents/blastdb.html">http://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast/documents/blastdb.html</a>). Hits to the two contaminant databases were identified and used to remove sequences that were largely contaminant, or to trim those with non-contaminant sequences at least 10kb long. After this step, 3,252 primary and 5,939 alternative contigs were retained. This set of contaminant-free contigs were next polished using quiver (version 2.3.0–140936) and default parameters. After this last step, 3,073 primary and 5,901 alternative contigs remained.</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec011" name="sec011" class="link-target" title="Illumina library construction and sequencing"></a>
<h3>Illumina library construction and sequencing</h3>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec012" name="sec012" class="link-target" title="Genomic libraries"></a><h4>Genomic libraries.</h4><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Approximately 1.5 μg of genomic DNA was used for the construction of Illumina genomic libraries; the DNA was fragmented using NEBNext dsDNA Fragmentase (New England BioLabs; NEB) for 40–60 min at 37°C and cleaned using Agencourt AMPure XP (Beckman Coulter Genomics, Tokyo, Japan) for size selection. To select fragments ranging between 300 and 600 bp, 27 μl of AMPure was added to the 63 μl reaction. After a brief incubation at RT, 90 μl of the supernatant was transferred to a new tube and 20 μl water and 30 μl AMPure were added. After a second brief incubation at RT, the supernatant was discarded and the DNA was eluted from the beads in 20 μl of water, as recommended. Next, DNA fragments were subjected to end repair using NEB’s End Repair Module Enzyme Mix, and A-base overhangs were added with Klenow (NEB), as recommended by the manufacturer. A-base addition was followed by AMPure cleanup using 1.8:1 (v/v) AMPure reaction. Barcoded NEXTflex adaptors (Bioo Scientific, Austin, USA) were ligated at room temperature using NEB Quick Ligase (NEB) following the manufacturer’s recommendations. To remove contamination of self-ligated adapter dimers, libraries were size-selected using AMPure in 0.8:1 (v/v) AMPure:reaction volume to select for adapter-ligated DNA fragments at least 400-bp long. Half of the eluted DNA was enriched by PCR reaction using Prime STAR Max (Takara, Tokyo, Japan) at the following PCR conditions: 30 s at 98°C, 10 cycles of 10 s at 98°C, 30 s at 65°C and 30 s at 72°C and a final extension step of 5 min at 72°C. Enriched libraries were purified with AMPure (0.7:1 v/v AMPure to reaction volume), and quality and quantity were assessed using the Agilent BioAnalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Tokyo, Japan) and Qubit fluorometer (Invitrogen, Waltham, USA). Libraries were sequenced using Illumina’s HiSeq 2500 or HiSeq4000 (150-bp paired-end reads).</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec013" name="sec013" class="link-target" title="GBS/ddRAD-Seq libraries"></a><h4>GBS/ddRAD-Seq libraries.</h4><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Two F1 mapping populations, derived from crosses between two <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em>, Kunsenshi-male and Kunsenshi-female, and between two <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em>, Kunsenshi-male and Budogaki-female, were employed for ddRAD-Seq [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref032" class="ref-tip">32</a>] and GBS [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref033" class="ref-tip">33</a>] analyses to construct genetic linkage maps. The former and latter mapping populations were named KK (n = 314) and VM (n = 119), respectively. Genomic DNA was extracted from the leaves of each line using the CTAB method. The ddRAD-Seq libraries for KK and VM were constructed using restriction enzymes <em>PstI</em> and <em>Msp</em>I [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref034" class="ref-tip">34</a>], while the GBS library for KK were prepared using with <em>Pst</em>I [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref033" class="ref-tip">33</a>].</p>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec014" name="sec014" class="link-target" title="mRNA libraries"></a><h4>mRNA libraries.</h4><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Developing buds and flowers from two <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> individuals, Kunsenshi-male and Kunsenshi-female, were harvested from June to April to cover the annual cycle of leaves/flower development. Total RNA was extracted using the Plant RNA Reagent (Invitrogen) and purified by phenol/chloroform extraction. Five micrograms of total RNA was processed in preparation for Illumina Sequencing, according to a previous report [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. In brief, mRNA was purified using the Dynabeads mRNA purification kit (Life Technologies, Tokyo, Japan). Next, cDNA was synthesized via random priming using Superscript III (Life Technologies) followed by heat inactivation for 5 min at 65°C. Second-strand cDNA was synthesized using the second-strand buffer (200 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, 22 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub> and 425 mM KCl), DNA polymerase I (NEB, Ipswich, USA) and RNaseH (NEB) with incubation at 16°C for 2.5 h. Double-stranded cDNA was purified using AMPure with a 0.7:1 (v/v) AMPure to reaction volume ratio. The resulting double-stranded cDNA was subjected to fragmentation and library construction, as described above, for genomic library preparation. Ten cycles of PCR enrichment were performed using the method described above. The constructed libraries were sequenced on Illumina’s HiSeq 4000 sequencer (50-bp single-end reads).</p>
</div>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec015" name="sec015" class="link-target" title="DAP-Seq libraries"></a><h4>DAP-Seq libraries.</h4><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The DAP genomic DNA libraries were prepared as previously described [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref025" class="ref-tip">25</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref028" class="ref-tip">28</a>, <a href="#pgen.1008566.ref035" class="ref-tip">35</a>]. Briefly, the Covaris M220 ultrasonicator (with the manufacturer-recommended setting) was used to fragment gDNA to an average size of 200 bp. The resulting fragmented gDNA was ligated to the NEXTflex adaptors (Bioo Scientific, Austin, USA) as described, to make genomic libraries. The full-length <em>SiMeGI</em> cDNA was cloned into the pDONR221 vector (Life Technologies) and then transferred to the pIX-Halo using LR clonase II (Life Technologies) to generate pIX-Halo-SiMeGI. pIX-Halo-MeGI has been constructed previously [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref025" class="ref-tip">25</a>]. The N-terminally Halo-tagged <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> were produced using the TNT SP6 Coupled Wheat Germ Extract System (Promega, Fitchburg, WI, USA) and purified with Magne HaloTag beads (Promega). A total of 50 ng DAP gDNA library was incubated with Halo-tagged <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> at room temperature for 1 h.</p>
</div>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec016" name="sec016" class="link-target" title="Sequencing"></a><h4>Sequencing.</h4><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The ddRAD Seq sequences were obtained at the Kazusa DNA Research Institute. The GBS sequences were obtained from the Genomic Diversity Facility (Cornell University). All other Illumina sequencing were conducted at the Vincent J. Coates Genomics Sequencing Laboratory at UC Berkeley, and the raw sequencing reads were processed using custom Python scripts developed in the Comai laboratory and available online (<a href="http://comailab.genomecenter.ucdavis.edu/index.php/Barcoded_data_preparation_tools">http://comailab.genomecenter.ucdavis.edu/index.php/Barcoded_data_preparation_tools</a>), as previously described. In brief, reads were split based on index information and trimmed for quality (average Phred sequence quality &gt; 20 over a 5 bp sliding window) and adaptor sequence contamination. A read length cut-off of 35 bps was applied to mRNA reads. Sequencing analysis of ddRAD-Seq libraries was performed at the Kazusa DNA Research Institute, and data processing was conducted as described in Shirasawa et al. [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref034" class="ref-tip">34</a>].</p>
</div>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec017" name="sec017" class="link-target" title="Gene prediction and genome/genes annotation"></a>
<h3>Gene prediction and genome/genes annotation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The RNA-Seq data for gene prediction was obtained from developing buds and flowers from <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> Kunsenshi-male at the following eight time points in 2013 to 2015 (June, July, August, October, January, March, early April, and late April) to cover the annual cycle of leaves/flower development. The RNA-Seq reads were trimmed according to previous reports [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. The cleaned reads were mapped onto the scaffolds of DLO_r1.1 using TopHat 2.0.14 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref036" class="ref-tip">36</a>], and the BAM files obtained were used for BRAKER1 1.9 pipeline [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref037" class="ref-tip">37</a>]. In the pipeline, GeneMark-ET 4.32 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref038" class="ref-tip">38</a>] and Augustus 3.1 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref039" class="ref-tip">39</a>] were used to construct the training set, and Augustus 3.1 was used for the gene prediction, using the training set. Genes were compared to the UniProtKB (<a href="http://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/">http://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/</a>) and of Araport11 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref040" class="ref-tip">40</a>] peptide sequences using BLASTP with E-value cutoff of 1E-10. Genes that were similar to those in the databases were categorized as “highly confident” (HC). Analysis of the conservation of the single-copy genes was conducted using BUSCO v1 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref041" class="ref-tip">41</a>]. Repeat sequences were detected using RepeatScout 1.0.5 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref042" class="ref-tip">42</a>] and RepeatMasker 4.0.6 (<a href="http://www.repeatmasker.org/">http://www.repeatmasker.org</a>) against the Repbase database [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref043" class="ref-tip">43</a>], according to the method used previously [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref044" class="ref-tip">44</a>]. The HC genes on the primary scaffolds (DLO_r1.1 primary) were compared to the genes of <em>Actinidia chinensis</em> (kiwifruit; 39,040 genes [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>]), <em>Vitis vinifera</em> (grape; 29,927 genes (IGGP 12x.31) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref045" class="ref-tip">45</a>]), <em>Solanum lycopersicum</em> (tomato; 34,789 genes (ITAG 3.10) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref012" class="ref-tip">12</a>]) and <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> (27,655 genes (Araport11)) using OrthoMCL 2.0.9. To estimate the divergence time between <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em>, <em>A</em>. <em>chinensis</em>, <em>V</em>. <em>vinifera</em>, and <em>A</em>. <em>thaliana</em>, the single copy genes conserved amongst all four species were aligned by MUSCLE 3.8.31 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref046" class="ref-tip">46</a>]. InDels in the alignment were eliminated using Gblocks 0.91b [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref047" class="ref-tip">47</a>], and the sequences were concatenated by species and used to construct the phylogenetic tree using the Maximum Likelihood method using MEGA 7.0.26 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref048" class="ref-tip">48</a>] with the Jones-Taylor-Thornton (JTT) model as the substitution model. The divergence time was estimated based on that between <em>A</em>. <em>chinensis</em> and <em>V</em>. <em>vinifera</em> (117 MYA) published in TIMETREE (<a href="http://www.timetree.org/">http://www.timetree.org</a>).</p>
</div>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec018" name="sec018" class="link-target" title="Construction of the persimmon database"></a>
<h3>Construction of the persimmon database</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The sequence data obtained was released in the form of the PersimmonDB (<a href="http://persimmon.kazusa.or.jp/">http://persimmon.kazusa.or.jp</a>). In the database, BLAST searches can be conducted against the scaffolds (DLO_r1.0) and pseudomolecules (DLO_r1.0_pseudomolecules), cds (DLO_r1.1_cds), and pep (DLO_r1.1_pep). Keyword searches are available against the results of the similarity searches against TrEMBL and peptide sequences in Araport11. The genomic and genic sequences, GFF files of the scaffolds and pseudomolecules, and BED files can be downloaded from the database. The scaffolds are also available under accession numbers BEWH01000001-BEWH01008975 (8,975 entries) in DDBJ. The raw sequence data is also available from under accession numbers DRA006168 (Illumina WGS for <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> Kunsenshi-male and female), DRA006169-DRA006176 (ddRAD-Seq/GBS for KK and VM populations), DRA006177 (RNA-Seq for <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> Kunsenshi-male), and DRA006182-DRA006184 (PacBio WGS for <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> Kunsenshi-male) in DDBJ.</p>
</div>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec019" name="sec019" class="link-target" title="Genetic anchoring of the scaffold using two mapping populations"></a>
<h3>Genetic anchoring of the scaffold using two mapping populations</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The sequence reads from the ddRAD-Seq and GBS libraries were mapped onto the primary contigs of the DLO_r1.0 reference sequence using Bowtie 2 (version 2.2.3) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref049" class="ref-tip">49</a>]. SNP calling was performed using the mpileup command of SAMtools (version 0.1.19) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref050" class="ref-tip">50</a>] and the view command of BCFtools [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref050" class="ref-tip">50</a>]. High-confidence SNPs were selected using VCFtools (version 0.1.12b) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref051" class="ref-tip">51</a>] using the following parameters: ≥10× coverage of each sample (—minDP 10); &gt;999 SNP quality value (—minQ 999); ≥0.2 minor allele frequency (—maf 0.2), and &lt;0.5 missing data rate (—max-missing 0.5). Totals of 3,535 and 4,027 high-confident SNPs were obtained in the KK and VM populations, respectively. Genotype information for all lines were prepared for the CP mode of JoinMap (version 4) and classified into groups using the Grouping Module of JoinMap with LOD scores of 4 to 7. Marker order and relative map distances were calculated using its regression-mapping algorithm with the following parameters: Haldane’s mapping function ≤0.35 recombination frequency, and  ≥2.0 LOD score. LPmerge (version 1.5) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref052" class="ref-tip">52</a>] was used to integrate the linkage maps into a single consensus map. To construct pseudomolecule sequences, scaffolds assigned to the genetic map for the Kunsenshi-male, the cultivar used for the genome sequencing analysis, were ordered and oriented in accordance with marker order if at least two marker loci were mapped on a single scaffold. Otherwise, in the cases of a single marker on a scaffold, the orientation of the sequence was determined as “unknown”.</p>
</div>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec020" name="sec020" class="link-target" title="Genomic characterization of the male-specific region of the Y-chromosome"></a>
<h3>Genomic characterization of the male-specific region of the Y-chromosome</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>For the male-specific regions of the Y-chromosome (MSY), including <em>OGI</em>, which was not anchored into Chr. 15 with SNPs markers, we aligned the candidate scaffolds using BAC-end sequences walking from the seed BAC clone that including <em>OGI</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. BAC library construction and screening were described previously [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. A total of 14 BAC clones were isolated to connect each other, and anchor 7 scaffolds surrounding <em>OGI</em>. The genomic reads of 10 male and 10 female individuals from the KK population [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>] were mapped to these sequences using BWA with the default mismatch allowance to determine reads coverage, and with no mismatch allowed to define the allele type (X or Y). In this study, &gt;3kb sequences were defined as pseudo-autosomal region (PAR)-like sequences, based on the fact that &gt; 30% of mapped reads were female (excluding repetitive sequences).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>To investigate the timing of divergence (or suppression of recombination) between the X and Y alleles of the genes located within the MSY and within the seven anchored scaffolds, X-allelic sequences were reconstructed by mapping the Illumina reads from female individuals from the KK population, to the Y-allelic reference sequences. The X- and Y-allelic sequences were aligned with MAFFT ver. 7 and analyzed with DnaSP 5.1 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref053" class="ref-tip">53</a>] to detect <em>dS</em> values between X-Y alleles. To standardize the estimated timing of divergence, the interspecific <em>dS</em> values between the X-alleles in <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> and three <em>Diospyros</em> species (<em>D</em>. <em>kaki</em>, <em>D</em>. <em>virginiana</em>, and <em>D</em>. <em>mespiliformis</em>; [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]) were assessed using DnaSP 5.1. The X-Y allelic <em>dS</em> were also measured in the recombining regions of the sex chromosomes (Dlo_pri0017F.1 and Dlo_pri0114F.1).</p>
</div>

<div id="section7" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec021" name="sec021" class="link-target" title="Comparative genomics"></a>
<h3>Comparative genomics</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec7.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec7.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Whole genome-resequencing analysis on the Kunsenshi-male and female individuals were performed as described in Shirasawa et al. (2017) [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref054" class="ref-tip">54</a>]. Paired-end sequences reads were obtained from the male and female lines with Illumina NextSeq, and trimmed and filtered based on quality score using Prinseq [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref055" class="ref-tip">55</a>] and base similarity to adapter sequences, AGATCGGAAGAGC, using fastx_clipper in the FASTX-Toolkit (<a href="http://hannonlab.cshl.edu/fastx_toolkit">http://hannonlab.cshl.edu/fastx_toolkit</a>). The resulting reads were mapped on the primary contigs of DLO_r1.0 reference sequence with Bowtie2, and single nucleotide polymorphisms were detected with SAMtools mpileup [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref050" class="ref-tip">50</a>] and filtered with the conditions of sequence depth of ≥10 in each line (—minDP 10) and mapping quality of &gt;200 in each SNP locus (—minQ 200) using VCFtools [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref051" class="ref-tip">51</a>]. The effect of SNPs on gene function were predicted with SnpEff [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref056" class="ref-tip">56</a>] to assign the SNPs to four impact categories, high, moderate, modifier, and low, predifined by SnpEff. Synteny relationship of the genome structures were predicted with PROmer program of Mummer package [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref057" class="ref-tip">57</a>] between <em>Diospyros</em> (this study) and <em>Actinidia</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>], as well as within the <em>Diospyros lotus</em> genomic fragments. The results were filtered using delta-filter and default parameters, and options of -i 20 -u 20. The results were visualized using Mummerplot or Circos [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref058" class="ref-tip">58</a>]. Collinearity of homologous genes (&lt;e<sup>-100</sup> in blastp analysis) was visualized using Strudel [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref059" class="ref-tip">59</a>]. Collinearity of gene order was analyzed and visualized using SynMap and GEvo in CoGe [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref020" class="ref-tip">20</a>] (<a href="https://genomevolution.org/coge/">https://genomevolution.org/coge/</a>).</p>
</div>

<div id="section8" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec022" name="sec022" class="link-target" title="Detection of genetic diversity within paralogs"></a>
<h3>Detection of genetic diversity within paralogs</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec8.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec8.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Genes annotated as potential transposable elements by blastn/blastp using the TAIR/nr databases, and potentially repetitive genes which produced &gt;5 homologous genes in the <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> genome (&lt;e<sup>-20</sup> in blastp), were discarded. <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> gene pairs showing significant sequence similarity (&lt;e<sup>-20</sup> in blastp), and their orthologs from three species, <em>Actinidia</em>, <em>Solanum</em> and <em>Vitis</em>, which were nested into the same gene family according to OrthoMCL results [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref060" class="ref-tip">60</a>], were subjected to in-codon frame alignment using their protein and nucleotide sequences with Pal2Nal and MAFFT ver. 7 under the L-INS-i model. The resulting alignments were subjected to Mega v.6 to estimate the Jukes and Cantor corrected values of synonymous (<em>dS</em>) and non-synonymous (<em>dN</em>) substitutions and the index of evolutionary rate (<em>dN/dS</em>). The four-fold degenerative sites were extracted from the alignments with PAML (icode = 11), and their pairwise transversion rates (4DTv) were calculated according to previous reports [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref061" class="ref-tip">61</a>]. To estimate the divergence time between the gene pairs, we adopted an estimated rate of 2.81 × 10<sup>−9</sup> substitutions per synonymous site per year, according to the report in <em>Actinidia</em> [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref062" class="ref-tip">62</a>].</p>
</div>

<div id="section9" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec023" name="sec023" class="link-target" title="Evolutionary analysis on the paralogs derived from Dd-α WGD event"></a>
<h3>Evolutionary analysis on the paralogs derived from <em>Dd-α</em> WGD event</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>To search for signs of positive selection, aligned nucleotide sequences of each gene pair and an outgroup ortholog, from either the <em>Actinidia</em>, <em>Solunum</em> or <em>Vitis</em> genomes, were subjected to codon-based detection of positive selection test using PAML [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref063" class="ref-tip">63</a>]. The statistical significance of positive selection on branches was evaluated using the likelihood ratio test of the null hypothesis that <em>dN/dS</em> = 1. Site-specific positive selection was assessed by Bayes Empirical Bayes analysis. To examine the positively selected sites common across the all three outgroups, in-frame alignments of the <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> gene pairs with the orthologs from all of the <em>Actinidia</em>, <em>Solanum</em> or <em>Vitis</em> genomes were used for the construction of evolutionary topologies using ML method by Mega v. 6, using the general time reversible (+I+G) model. Based on these alignments and topology, the branch- and site-specific positive selection test was performed using PAML, as well.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>To define the phylogenetic relationship between the <em>MeGI</em>/<em>SiMeGI</em>-like orthologs/paralogs in angiosperms, genes showing significant homology (&lt;1e<sup>-10</sup> in blastp analysis) to a HD-ZIP1 OsHOX4 from <em>Oryza sativa</em>, which was previously used as the outgroup gene for the <em>MeGI</em> clade [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>], were collected from the <em>Diospyros lotus</em>, <em>Solanum lycopersicum</em>, <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>, <em>Oryza sativa</em>, and <em>Zea mays</em> genomes. A total of 174 protein sequences from these genomes, and that of Vrs1 from barley [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>] were aligned using MAFFT ver. 7, followed by manual pruning with SeaView. The pruned alignment was subjected to the NJ approach using Mega v. 6, with the JTT model, to construct phylogenetic tree (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s006">S6 Fig</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec9.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>To assess selective pressure on <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em>, their alleles from other members of the Ebenaceae family (<em>Diospyros</em> and <em>Euclea</em> genera), and their orthologs in the <em>Actinidia</em>, <em>Solunum</em> or <em>Vitis</em> genomes were subjected to in-codon frame alignment by MAFFT ver. 7, followed by a ML approach using Mega v. 6, with HKY+G model, to construct an evolutionary topology. The putative ancestral sequences of the <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> origins in the Ebenaceae family, and the sequences in the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) of the order Ericale and of the Asterids, were estimated using Mega. Informative SNPs in the aligned sequences were analyzed by DnaSP 5.1 [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref053" class="ref-tip">53</a>] and used to calculate a series of window-average <em>dN/dS</em> values, from the start codon (ATG) in a 150-bp window with a 30-bp step size, until the walking window reached the stop codon.To assess differentiation of expression patterns between the <em>Dd-α</em>-derived paralog pairs, we conducted Pearson’s product moment correlation analysis and Fisher’s exact test. Differentiation between the developmental stages of the buds/flowers throughout the annual cycle was examined by the “cor.test” function in R (with “pearson” method), using mRNA-Seq transcriptome data from datapoints (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s028">S3 Dataset</a>). Differentiation of expression pattern between male and female flowers was examined for each paralog pair using a 2x2 Fisher’s exact test (“fisher.test” function in R), and using mRNA-Seq transcriptome data from early developing stage and maturing stage, respectively (<a href="#pgen.1008566.s028">S3 Dataset</a>).</p>
</div>

<div id="section10" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec024" name="sec024" class="link-target" title="Transformation of MeGI and SiMeGI"></a>
<h3>Transformation of <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em></h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec10.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec10.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Full length sequences of the <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> transcripts were amplified by PCR using PrimeSTAR Max (TaKaRa) from cDNA synthesized from RNA, itself derived from developing flower buds of <em>D</em>. <em>lotus</em> cv. Kunsenshi-male. The amplicons were cloned into the pGWB2 vector to place the genes under the control of CaMV35S promoter. We constructed pGWB2-<em>MeGI</em> and pGWB2-<em>SiMeGI</em> using the Gateway system (Invitrogen) and the pENTR/D-TOPO cloning kit and LR clonase. Tobacco plants (<em>N</em>. <em>tabacum</em>) cv. Petit Havana SR1 were grown <em>in vitro</em> under white light with 16-h-light and 8-h-dark cycles at 22°C until transformation. The binary construct was introduced into the <em>A</em>. <em>tumefaciens</em> strain EHA101. Young petioles and leaves of tobacco plants were transformed by the leaf disk method as previously described [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. Transgenic plants were selected on Murashige and Skoog medium supplemented with 100 μg/mL kanamycin. Pollen tube germination was assessed 6 h after placing the pollen grains on 15% sucrose/0.005% boric acid/1.0% agarose media at 25°C. The pollen germination ratio was counted as average percentages in batches of 200 pollen grains from the first three flowers.</p>
</div>

<div id="section11" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec025" name="sec025" class="link-target" title="RNA in situ hybridization"></a>
<h3>RNA <em>in situ</em> hybridization</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec11.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec11.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>RNA <em>in situ</em> hybridization was performed as previously described [<a href="#pgen.1008566.ref064" class="ref-tip">64</a>], but with minor modifications. Briefly, bud samples were fixed in FAA (1.8% formaldehyde, 5% acetic acid, 50% ethanol), dehydrated using an ethanol: t-butanol series, and then embedded in paraffin. The embedded tissues were sliced into ca 10-μm sections, and the sections were mounted on FRONTIER coated glass slide (Matsunami Glass Ind., Japan). Paraffin was removed with xylene, and the tissue sections were rehydrated in an ethanol series. The tissue sections were then incubated in a Proteinase K solution (700U/mL Proteinase K, 50mM EDTA, 0.1M Tris-HCl pH 7.5) for 30 min at 37°C, followed by acetylation with acetic anhydride (0.25% acetic anhydride in 0.1 M triethanolamine solution) for 10 min. Full length <em>MeGI</em> and <em>SiMeGI</em> cDNA sequences were cloned into the pGEM-T Easy vector (Promega, WI, USA) to synthesize the DIG-labelled probes, respectively. Antisense RNA probes were synthesized using the DIG-labeling RNA synthesis kit (Roche, Switzerland), according to the manufacturer’s instruction. The probe solution including RNaseOUT (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, USA) was applied to the slides and covered with parafilm. Hybridization was performed at 48°C for &gt;16 h. For detection, 0.1% Anti-Digoxigenin-AP Fab fragments (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, USA) was used as the secondary antibody to stain with NBT/BCIP solutions.</p>
</div>
