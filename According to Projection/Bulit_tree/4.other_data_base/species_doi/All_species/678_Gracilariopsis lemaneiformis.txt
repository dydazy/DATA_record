Despite it is economic importance, it has remained largely unstudied at the genomic level. In this study, we conducted a genome survey of Gp. lemaneiformis using next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies. In total, 18.70 Gb of high-quality sequence data with an estimated genome size of 97 Mb were obtained by HiSeq 2000 sequencing for Gp. lemaneiformis. These reads were assembled into 160,390 contigs with a N50 length of 3.64 kb, which were further assembled into 125,685 scaffolds with a total length of 81.17 Mb. Genome analysis predicted 3490 genes and a GC% content of 48%. The identified genes have an average transcript length of 1,429 bp, an average coding sequence size of 1,369 bp, 1.36 exons per gene, exon length of 1,008 bp, and intron length of 191 bp. From the initial assembled scaffold, transposable elements constituted 54.64% (44.35 Mb) of the genome, and 7737 simple sequence repeats (SSRs) were identified. Among these SSRs, the trinucleotide repeat type was the most abundant (up to 73.20% of total SSRs), followed by the di- (17.41%), tetra- (5.49%), hexa- (2.90%), and penta- (1.00%) nucleotide repeat type. These characteristics suggest that Gp. lemaneiformis is a model organism for genetic study. This is the first report of genome-wide characterization within this taxon." />

<div class="abstract toc-section abstract-type-"><a id="abstract0" name="abstract0" data-toc="abstract0" class="link-target" title="Abstract"></a><h2>Abstract</h2><div class="abstract-content"><a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p><em>Gracilariopsis lemaneiformis</em> has a high economic value and is one of the most important aquaculture species in China. Despite it is economic importance, it has remained largely unstudied at the genomic level. In this study, we conducted a genome survey of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> using next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies. In total, 18.70 Gb of high-quality sequence data with an estimated genome size of 97 Mb were obtained by HiSeq 2000 sequencing for <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em>. These reads were assembled into 160,390 contigs with a N50 length of 3.64 kb, which were further assembled into 125,685 scaffolds with a total length of 81.17 Mb. Genome analysis predicted 3490 genes and a GC% content of 48%.</p>
<a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p2" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The identified genes have an average transcript length of 1,429 bp, an average coding sequence size of 1,369 bp, 1.36 exons per gene, exon length of 1,008 bp, and intron length of 191 bp. From the initial assembled scaffold, transposable elements constituted 54.64% (44.35 Mb) of the genome, and 7737 simple sequence repeats (SSRs) were identified. Among these SSRs, the trinucleotide repeat type was the most abundant (up to 73.20% of total SSRs), followed by the di- (17.41%), tetra- (5.49%), hexa- (2.90%), and penta- (1.00%) nucleotide repeat type. These characteristics suggest that <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> is a model organism for genetic study. This is the first report of genome-wide characterization within this taxon.</p>
</div></div>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="s1" name="s1" data-toc="s1" class="link-target" title="Introduction"></a><h2>Introduction</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p><em>Gracilariopsis</em> was long indistinguishable from the genus <em>Gracilaria</em> in the family Gracilariaceae Nageli (Rhodophyta). In 1989, however, Fredericq and Hommersand <a href="#pone.0069909-Fredericq1" class="ref-tip">[1]</a> reinstated this genus based on four important differences between the two genera. Considered to be one of the most important economic macroalgae, <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> Bory is utilized for agar extraction <a href="#pone.0069909-Lapointe1" class="ref-tip">[2]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-MarinhoSoriano1" class="ref-tip">[3]</a>. Its high agar yield accounts for more than half of total annual agar production worldwide <a href="#pone.0069909-McHugh1" class="ref-tip">[4]</a>. <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> is also an ideal material for genetic research <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhang1" class="ref-tip">[5]</a>, and it has a potential role in the inhibition of red tides and in bioremediation <a href="#pone.0069909-Wang1" class="ref-tip">[6]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhou1" class="ref-tip">[7]</a>. Single-rope floating raft cultivation of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> has been widespread along the coastline of South China since the 1990s <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhang1" class="ref-tip">[5]</a>. Currently, <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> is one of the most important aquaculture species in China <a href="#pone.0069909-Yang1" class="ref-tip">[8]</a>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>Following the success of the human genome project, next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies were launched. Compared with Roche's 454 and ABI's SOLiD, HiSeq 2000 offers the cheapest sequencing and the biggest output <a href="#pone.0069909-Liu1" class="ref-tip">[9]</a>. Illumina HiSeq 2000 also has been successfully used to characterize the genetic background of many marine species, including <em>Saccharina japonica</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Deng1" class="ref-tip">[10]</a>, <em>Pyropia yezoensis</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Nakamura1" class="ref-tip">[11]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[12]</a>, <em>Membranipora grandicella</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Shen1" class="ref-tip">[13]</a>, <em>Anguilla japonica</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Henkel1" class="ref-tip">[14]</a>, <em>Pinctada fucata</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Huang1" class="ref-tip">[15]</a>, <em>Pinctada martensii</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhao1" class="ref-tip">[16]</a> and <em>Pseudomonas stutzeri</em> AN10 <a href="#pone.0069909-BrunetGalms1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p3" name="article1.body1.sec1.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>Genetic studies of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> aimed at improving its agar yield and growth rate are relatively new. Recent genetic studies have focused on the mitochondrial genome <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>, functional gene cloning <a href="#pone.0069909-Bojsen1" class="ref-tip">[19]</a>, analysis of genetic diversity <a href="#pone.0069909-Pang1" class="ref-tip">[20]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Wang2" class="ref-tip">[21]</a>, and mutation research <a href="#pone.0069909-Meng1" class="ref-tip">[22]</a>. However, genetic studies of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> remain underdeveloped compared with many other aquaculture species, such as <em>S. japonica</em>, <em>P. yezoensis</em>, <em>Cynoglossus semilaevis</em>, and <em>Apostichopus japonicus</em>, which might be due in part to the insufficient genetic or genomic resources available for <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em>. To investigate and provide a genomic resource for further research (e.g., molecular cloning, structural and functional genomic studies, breeding, and comparative and evolutionary studies) on this species, we conducted a genome survey of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> using NGS technology. The results of this study should be useful for crop improvement programs and better utilization of the existing genomic information in the future.</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2" name="s2" data-toc="s2" class="link-target" title="Materials and Methods"></a><h2>Materials and Methods</h2>
<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2a" name="s2a" class="link-target" title="Sample preparation"></a>
<h3>Sample preparation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>A healthy female thallus of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em>, which was identified based on its morphology at maturity <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhang3" class="ref-tip">[23]</a>, was collected in November 2011 from the intertidal zone of Zhanshan Bay, Qingdao, China (36°02′ N, 120°20′ E). After clearing the specimen in seawater to remove epiphytes, mud, and sand, the thalli were rinsed with 1% sodium hypochlorite for 2 min and treated in antibiotic seawater containing 0.3 g L<sup>−1</sup> penicillin, 0.2 g L<sup>−1</sup> nystatin, 0.02 g L<sup>−1</sup> cefotaxine, 0.1 g L<sup>−1</sup> kanamycin, 1.0 g L<sup>−1</sup> streptomycin sulphate, and 0.1 g ml<sup>−1</sup> GeO<sub>2</sub> for about 6 h to remove possible bacterial contaminants and diatoms. Part of a thallus then was used for DNA extraction, and the remaining thalli were cultured continuously in modified Provasoli's medium <a href="#pone.0069909-Pflugmacher1" class="ref-tip">[24]</a> under the following conditions: temperature of 20°C, photon flux density of 15 µmol m<sup>−2</sup>s<sup>−1</sup>, salinity of 30‰, and a 12 h light∶12 h dark photoperiod regime.</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2b" name="s2b" class="link-target" title="DNA extraction, library construction, and sequencing"></a>
<h3>DNA extraction, library construction, and sequencing</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Genomic DNA was extracted using the Plant Genomic DNA Kit (Tiangenbiotech, Beijing, China) following the manufacturer's instructions. The quantity and quality of genomic DNA were measured using 1% agarose gel electrophoresis and Gene Quant (Amersham Bioscience, San Francisco, USA). Two paired-end libraries with insert sizes of about 170 base pairs (bp) and 500 bp were constructed from fragmented random genomic DNA following the manufacturer's instructions (Illumina, Guangzhou, China). Sequence data generation, using the Illumina HiSeq 2000 sequencing platform was conducted by Beijing Genomics Institute (Shenzhen, China). All sequencing reads were deposited in the Short Read Archive (SRA) database (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra/">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra/</a>), which are retrievable under the accession number SRX258772.</p>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2c" name="s2c" class="link-target" title="Sequence assembly and analysis"></a>
<h3>Sequence assembly and analysis</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The raw HiSeq 2000 reads were first pre-processed by trimming short tips and low quality sequences using a phred-scale quality score cut-off of 20 for the acquisition of Q20 values. Genome assembly was completed with the pre-processed reads using SOAPdenovo software <a href="#pone.0069909-Li1" class="ref-tip">[25]</a>. All usable reads were realigned to the contig sequences, and then the paired-end relationship between reads was aligned between contigs. We constructed the scaffolds step by step using variant insert size paired-ends. Default assembly parameters of &gt;40 bp overlap length and &gt;90% sequence identity were used. The genome size was calculated using the total length of sequence reads and sequencing depth <a href="#pone.0069909-Varshney1" class="ref-tip">[26]</a>.</p>
</div>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2d" name="s2d" class="link-target" title="Analysis of G+C content"></a>
<h3>Analysis of G+C content</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>We used 10-kb non-overlapping sliding windows along the assembled sequence to calculate GC content and average sequencing depth. The x-axis was GC content percent across every 10-kb window and the y-axis was average sequencing depth, which was determined across every 10-kb window independently.</p>
</div>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2e" name="s2e" class="link-target" title="Identification of repeat sequences"></a>
<h3>Identification of repeat sequences</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Tandem repeats and interspersed repeats are two main types of repeats in the genome. The program TRF was used to search for tandem repeats. The annotated interspersed repeats were identified using RepeatMasker (version 3.3.0) and RepeatProteinMasker against the Repbase transposable element library (version 16.10). The software programs LTR-FINDER, Piler, and RepeatScout were used to construct a <em>de novo</em> repeat library, and then the software RepeatMasker was run to find homolog repeats in the <em>de novo</em> repeat library <a href="#pone.0069909-Jurka1" class="ref-tip">[27]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Price1" class="ref-tip">[28]</a>.</p>
</div>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2f" name="s2f" class="link-target" title="Gene prediction, annotation and comparison"></a>
<h3>Gene prediction, annotation and comparison</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Homology-based and de novo methods were used to predict genes, and the predicted results were integrated by the program GLEAN 17 <a href="#pone.0069909-Elsik1" class="ref-tip">[29]</a>. Protein sequences from <em>Chondrus crispus</em>, <em>Cyanidioschyzon merolae</em>, <em>Pyropia yezoensis</em>, <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>, <em>Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</em>, <em>Chlorella variabilis</em>, and <em>Oryza sativa</em> were mapped to the genome assembly using blastN with an E-value of 1e-5 <a href="#pone.0069909-Kent1" class="ref-tip">[30]</a> to perform gene prediction, using one species each time. For de novo prediction, Augustus <a href="#pone.0069909-Stanke1" class="ref-tip">[31]</a> and Genscan <a href="#pone.0069909-Salamov1" class="ref-tip">[32]</a> were used to predict genes with parameters trained on <em>A. thaliana</em>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>Each predict gene was annotated by blastP to the GenBank database. Functional assignments were mapped onto Gene Ontology (GO) using Blast2GO and then the proportions of GO categories among four red algae species were compared using WEGO (<a href="http://wego.genomics.org.cn/cgi-bin/wego/index.pl">http://wego.genomics.org.cn/cgi-bin/wego/index.pl</a>).</p>
</div>

<div id="section7" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2g" name="s2g" class="link-target" title="Synteny analysis of the genome"></a>
<h3>Synteny analysis of the genome</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The two reference genomes (<em>C. variabilis</em> and <em>A. thaliana</em>) were downloaded from <a href="ftp://ftp.ensemblgenomes.org/pub/release-15/plants/fasta/arabidopsis_thaliana/(A">ftp://ftp.ensemblgenomes.org/pub/release-15/plants/fasta/arabidopsis_thaliana/(A</a>. <em>thaliana</em>) and <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genome/694">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genome/694</a> (<em>C. variabilis</em>), respectively. We used BLASTP <a href="#pone.0069909-Bairoch1" class="ref-tip">[33]</a> with an E-value threshold of 1e-5 to identify homologous genes between <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> and these two reference genomes. The program MCscan (version 8.0) then was used to identify syntenic blocks between the two genomes.</p>
</div>

<div id="section8" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2h" name="s2h" class="link-target" title="Identification of simple sequence repeats (SSRs)"></a>
<h3>Identification of simple sequence repeats (SSRs)</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>SSRs were mined in the genome sequence using the SSRIT program with the following parameters: at least six repeats for di- and four repeats for tri-, tetra-, penta-, and hexa-nucleotides for SSRs.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3" name="s3" data-toc="s3" class="link-target" title="Results and Discussion"></a><h2>Results and Discussion</h2>

One of the most popular metrics to comparing assemblies is the N50 statistic <a href="#pone.0069909-Salzberg1" class="ref-tip">[37]</a>, which is defined as a weighted median and is the smallest contig size in the set whose combined length totals 50% of the genome assembly <a href="#pone.0069909-Carneiro1" class="ref-tip">[38]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Earl1" class="ref-tip">[39]</a>. A large N50 contig and contig number might simply reflect a continuous and complete assembly <a href="#pone.0069909-Li3" class="ref-tip">[41]</a>. However, it would be not valid to compare species using the contig N50 statistics from different assemblies if each N50 statistic was not calculated from the same combined length value <a href="#pone.0069909-Miller1" class="ref-tip">[44]</a>.</p>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3b" name="s3b" class="link-target" title="GC content and GC-depth analysis"></a>
<h3>GC content and GC-depth analysis</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Generally, genomic sequences generated through NGS are not uniformly distributed across the genome, as they are wider than the Poisson distribution <a href="#pone.0069909-Smith1" class="ref-tip">[45]</a>. GC content was one of three factors found to contribute to sequence bias from Illumina's platform <a href="#pone.0069909-Cheung1" class="ref-tip">[46]</a>. Compared with mid-GC content, high and low GC contents cause reduced coverage in sequencing regions <a href="#pone.0069909-Aird1" class="ref-tip">[47]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Bentley1" class="ref-tip">[48]</a>. To measure genome-wide sequencing bias, GC content and average sequencing depth were plotted using non-overlapping 10-kb sliding windows along the assembled sequence. The density points only concentrated in the 40–60% range, with the average GC content of ∼48%. Moreover, <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> had a mid-GC content <a href="#pone.0069909-Aird1" class="ref-tip">[47]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Bentley1" class="ref-tip">[48]</a>, which did not demonstrate abnormality of the species sample (some species themselves have abnormal GC content) or sequencing bias for the present data (<a href="#pone-0069909-g001">Figure 1</a>).</p>
<a class="link-target" id="pone-0069909-g001" name="pone-0069909-g001"></a><div class="figure" data-doi="10.1371/journal.pone.0069909.g001"><div class="img-box"><a title="Click for larger image" href="article/figure/image?size=medium&amp;id=info:doi/10.1371/journal.pone.0069909.g001" data-doi="info:doi/10.1371/journal.pone.0069909" data-uri="info:doi/10.1371/journal.pone.0069909.g001"><img src="article/figure/image?size=inline&amp;id=info:doi/10.1371/journal.pone.0069909.g001" alt="thumbnail" class="thumbnail" loading="lazy"></a><div class="expand"></div></div><div class="figure-inline-download">
          Download:

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3c" name="s3c" class="link-target" title="Estimation of genome size"></a>
<h3>Estimation of genome size</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Based on K-mer analysis, all sequences of about 18.70 Gb (<a href="#pone-0069909-t001">Table 1</a>) were used to estimate the genome size of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em>. The peak depth was at 162X, and we estimated the genome size to be 95.96 Mb. Generally, the 30X data are relatively accurate for estimating the size of the genome (based on evaluation experience of the Beijing Genomics Institute), so 3.5 Gb (18.70 Gb *30X/162X) of data were used to count and plot the distribution of 17-mer frequency. The peak depth was at 30X (<a href="#pone-0069909-g002">Figure 2</a>), and the number of 17-mers was 2,910,526,453 (<a href="#pone-0069909-t003">Table 3</a>). Their relationship could be expressed by the algorithm: Genome Size  =  K-mer_num/Peak_depth. The estimated genome size of 97.02 Mb (<a href="#pone-0069909-t003">Table 3</a>) was obtained, which was just one-quarter of the diploid size (375 Mb) for this alga based on average genetic substance content within the cells of <em>Gracilariopsis</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Zhang3" class="ref-tip">[23]</a>. The result also was not in accordance with the result of Kapraun and Dutcher <a href="#pone.0069909-Kapraun1" class="ref-tip">[34]</a>, who reported a nuclear genome size of 160 Mb (haploid) in <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> using microspectrophotometry with the DNA-localizing fluorochrome hydroethidine. The same fluorescence method had been used to evaluate the genome size of <em>Ch. crispus</em> and obtained 150-Mb genome <a href="#pone.0069909-Gall1" class="ref-tip">[53]</a>, which was about one-third larger than that of the complete genome sequencing method (105 Mb) <a href="#pone.0069909-Colln1" class="ref-tip">[56]</a>. We intend to verify the genome size using the flow cytometry method in the future. In total, haploid <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> appeared having a fairly small genome size, which would be an advantage for full sequencing of the genome.</p>

These features make <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> a good model for studying genetics and genomics. It might also prove to be a model organism for common analyses of algal genetics and molecular biology.</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3d" name="s3d" class="link-target" title="Repetitive sequences"></a>
<h3>Repetitive sequences</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Combined results from RepeatMasker and RepeatProteinMasker analyses revealed that transposable elements (TEs) constituted 54.64% (44.35 Mb) of the genome, and nearly one-third (20.46% of the genome) of them could not be classified within the TE regime (<a href="#pone-0069909-t004">Table 4</a>). Classification of the known TEs revealed that the majority of repeats were long-terminal repeat elements (LTRs) (26.40%), whereas 8.56% of the TEs were DNA transposons (<a href="#pone-0069909-t004">Table 4</a>).</p>

Repetitive sequences, especially TEs, are known to be prominent evolutionary factors that played a significant role in plant gene structure and genome evolution by enhancing genome plasticity, such as transposition, excision, insertion, chromosome breakage, and chromosome rearrangements <a href="#pone.0069909-Bennetzen1" class="ref-tip">[65]</a>–<a href="#pone.0069909-Wang4" class="ref-tip">[68]</a>.</p>

Repeated sequences constituted 73% of the <em>Ch. crispus</em> genome, ∼55% (representing 58 Mb) of which were LTR retrotransposons <a href="#pone.0069909-Colln1" class="ref-tip">[56]</a>. As in <em>Ch. crispus</em> and some higher plants such as maize <a href="#pone.0069909-Bennetzen2" class="ref-tip">[69]</a> and <em>Ricinus communis</em> <a href="#pone.0069909-Chan1" class="ref-tip">[70]</a>, the most abundant retrotransposons in <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> are LTRs, which might be associated with potential horizontal transfer, insertional mutations, and heterochromatin near centromeres <a href="#pone.0069909-Faulkner1" class="ref-tip">[71]</a>, <a href="#pone.0069909-Nagarajan1" class="ref-tip">[72]</a>.</p>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3e" name="s3e" class="link-target" title="Gene prediction, annotation and comparison"></a>
<h3>Gene prediction, annotation and comparison</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Both homology-based and de novo gene prediction methods were used to predict the number of genes in the <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> genome (<a href="#pone-0069909-t005">Table 5</a>). First, Genscan and Augustus predicted 3369 and 3363 gene loci, respectively. Next, four homologous genome sequences were mapped to the genome assembly. Predicted genes 1,858–4,393 were obtained with an average transcript length of 787.06–910.23 bp. Subsequently, all data were integrated using GLEAN 17, and 3,490 genes were identified with an average transcript length of 1,429 bp, average coding sequence size of 1,369 bp, 1.36 exons per gene, exon length of 1,008 bp, and intron length of 191 bp.</p>

Especially, the genes of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> also had few introns and nearly three quarters of predicted genes are monoexonic, resulting in a low average number of introns per gene (0.36). The high proportion of monoexonic genes for <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em>, which was comparable to those of other red algae, further verified Collén et al.'s conclusion that a low number of introns were a typical characteristic in red algal genomes <a href="#pone.0069909-Colln1" class="ref-tip">[56]</a>.</p>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3f" name="s3f" class="link-target" title="Synteny with sequenced plant genomes"></a>
<h3>Synteny with sequenced plant genomes</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>We compared about 81.17 Mb of the genomic sequence from <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> with the <em>A. thaliana</em> and <em>C. variabilis</em> genomes using the program LASTZ, and then MCscan was used to identify syntenic blocks. The genomic sequence of <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> showed minimal matching to <em>A. thaliana</em> (match length of 900,618 bp with only 1.11% coverage) and <em>C. variabilis</em> (match length of 345,067 bp with only 0.43% coverage),and the minimal matching could not be used to evaluate the general features of synteny relationships (<a href="#pone-0069909-t007">Tables 7</a> and <a href="#pone-0069909-t008">8</a>). Although it should be possible to analyze the evolutionary history between two species using complete genomic sequences <a href="#pone.0069909-Okuno1" class="ref-tip">[76]</a>, the following factors would make the process difficult or impossible: short sequence length of initial assembly, low gene distribution, and species specificity.</p>

<div id="section7" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3g" name="s3g" class="link-target" title="SSR detection"></a>
<h3>SSR detection</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec7.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec7.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>In this study, we found 7737 derived SSRs, none of which were mononucleotide repeats and complex SSR type. Among these SSRs, the trinucleotide repeat type was the most abundant (up to 73.20% of total SSRs), followed by di- (17.41%), tetra- (5.49%), hexa- (2.90%), and penta- (1.00%) nucleotide repeat types (<a href="#pone-0069909-g004">Figure 4</a>).</p>

Within the trinucleotide repeat motifs, the common motifs TGC/GCA and CAG/CTG accounted for 14.42% and 14.05%, respectively, in <em>Gp. lemaneiformis</em> (<a href="#pone-0069909-g006">Figure 6</a>).</p>
