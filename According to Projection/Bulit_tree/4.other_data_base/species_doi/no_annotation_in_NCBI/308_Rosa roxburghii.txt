<div class="abstract toc-section abstract-type-"><a id="abstract0" name="abstract0" data-toc="abstract0" class="link-target" title="Abstract"></a><h2>Abstract</h2><div class="abstract-content"><a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p><em>Rosa roxburghii</em> Tratt is an important commercial horticultural crop in China that is recognized for its nutritional and medicinal values. In spite of the economic significance, genomic information on this rose species is currently unavailable. In the present research, a genome survey of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was carried out using next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies. Total 30.29 Gb sequence data was obtained by HiSeq 2500 sequencing and an estimated genome size of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was 480.97 Mb, in which the guanine plus cytosine (GC) content was calculated to be 38.63%. All of these reads were technically assembled and a total of 627,554 contigs with a N50 length of 1.484 kb and furthermore 335,902 scaffolds with a total length of 409.36 Mb were obtained. Transposable elements (TE) sequence of 90.84 Mb which comprised 29.20% of the genome, and 167,859 simple sequence repeats (SSRs) were identified from the scaffolds. Among these, the mono-(66.30%), di-(25.67%), and tri-(6.64%) nucleotide repeats contributed to nearly 99% of the SSRs, and sequence motifs AG/CT (28.81%) and GAA/TTC (14.76%) were the most abundant among the dinucleotide and trinucleotide repeat motifs, respectively. Genome analysis predicted a total of 22,721 genes which have an average length of 2311.52 bp, an average exon length of 228.15 bp, and average intron length of 401.18 bp. Eleven genes putatively involved in ascorbate metabolism were identified and its expression in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> leaves was validated by quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR). This is the first report of genome-wide characterization of this rose species.</p>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec001" name="sec001" data-toc="sec001" class="link-target" title="Introduction"></a><h2>Introduction</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Presently, about 100–250 species have been described in the genus <em>Rosa</em>, many of which are recognized for their ornamental horticultural use [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref001" class="ref-tip">1</a>]. The chromosome number of members of this genus are based on multiples of seven and range from 2n = 2x = 14 to 2n = 8x = 56 [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref002" class="ref-tip">2</a>]. <em>Rosa roxburghii</em> Tratt (2n = 2x = 14), which is widely distributed in Southwest China, has aroused statewide interest for its wide range of nutritional and medicinal components in fruits as well as in leaves, including ascorbate (AsA), superoxide dismutase, flavonoids, and polysaccharides [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref003" class="ref-tip">3</a>–<a href="#pone.0147530.ref005" class="ref-tip">5</a>]. The economic cultivation area of this species in China involves at least 30,000 hectares, and a series of health care products has been developed for clinical applications.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>Despite its economic importance, the inheritance pattern of most agronomically significant traits of <em>Rosa roxburghii</em> has not yet been established. The limited genetic and genomic resources for this species have thus resulted in minimal improvement in its breeding programs. Collecting wild germplasm and selecting elite genotypes of this rosebush based on plant growth vigor and fruit characteristics started in the early 1980s in China [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref006" class="ref-tip">6</a>], and only one cultivar and some elite lines have been identified to date [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>]. Random amplification of polymorphic DNA (RAPD) and amplified fragment length polymorphism (AFLP) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref008" class="ref-tip">8</a>] markers have been employed to describe the genotypes of <em>Rosa roxburghii</em>. Recently, several SSR markers have been developed based on transcriptome sequencing [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>], but no genomic sequence-based markers are available for this species.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p3" name="article1.body1.sec1.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>AsA, also known as vitamin C, is of vital importance to plant cells as an antioxidant and enzyme cofactor [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref010" class="ref-tip">10</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref011" class="ref-tip">11</a>]. Several AsA biosynthetic pathways have been proposed in higher plants and the route that occurs via L-galactose has been well established [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref012" class="ref-tip">12</a>]. Recently, we identified and analyzed the candidate genes involved in the biosynthesis of AsA in the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> fruit based on the fruit transcriptome data [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>]; however, the metabolic mechanisms underlying AsA overproduction in this plant remain unknown. In addition, the level and distribution of AsA generally depends on both its synthesis as well as recycling [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref013" class="ref-tip">13</a>]. Biosynthesized AsA can be oxidized to mono-dehydroascorbate (MDA) and ultimately to dehydroascorbate (DHA) by the activities of ascorbate peroxidase (APX; EC 1.11.1.11) and ascorbate oxidase (AAO; EC 1.10.3.3). Then, part of the oxidized AsA is reduced back to AsA through the ascorbate–glutathione cycle by MDA reductase (MDAR; EC 1.6.5.4) and DHA reductase (DHAR; EC 1.8.5.1) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref011" class="ref-tip">11</a>]. The proposed AsA synthetic and recycling pathways were shown in the <a href="#pone-0147530-g001">Fig 1</a>.</p>

The recycling pathways were catalyzed by APX (ascorbate peroxidase), AAO (ascorbate oxidase), MDAR (mono-dehydroascorbate reductase) and DHAR (dehydroascorbate reductase).</p>
</p><p class="caption_object"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0147530.g001">
              https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0147530.g001</a></p></div><a id="article1.body1.sec1.p4" name="article1.body1.sec1.p4" class="link-target"></a><p>Following the success of the Human Genome Project, several Rosaceae species, including <em>Malus</em> × <em>domestica</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>], <em>Fragaria vesca</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>], <em>Prunus mume</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref016" class="ref-tip">16</a>], <em>Prunus persica</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>], <em>Pyrus bretschneideri</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>], and <em>Pyrus communis</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>] have been sequenced by using next-generation sequencing (NGS) technology. Genome survey sequencing via NGS is an important and cost-effective strategy in generating extensive genetic and genomic information relating to the metabolism and development of organisms. Therefore, to investigate and provide a genomic resource of this species, we conducted a genome survey of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> using NGS. Based on these data, we identified candidate genes involved in leaf AsA metabolism. The results of the present study contribute to accelerating the progress of gene discovery, genetic diversity, evolutionary analysis, structural genomic studies, and genetic improvement of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>, as well as its closely related species.</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec002" name="sec002" data-toc="sec002" class="link-target" title="Materials and Methods"></a><h2>Materials and Methods</h2>
<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec003" name="sec003" class="link-target" title="Plant materials"></a>
<h3>Plant materials</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Plants of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> ‘Guinong 5’ [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref007" class="ref-tip">7</a>] were grown in the fruit germplasm repository of Guizhou University, Guiyang, China (26°42.408'N, 106°67.353'E). Genomic DNAs were isolated from young leaf tissues of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> using a plant genomic DNA extraction kit (Tiangenbiotech, Beijing, China), following the manufacturer’s instructions. DNA quality and quantity were assessed by 1% agarose gel electrophoresis, and the concentrations of nucleic acids and proteins were measured on a BioPhotometer (Eppendorf, Germany).</p>
</div>

<div id="section2" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec004" name="sec004" class="link-target" title="Genome sequencing and genome size estimation"></a>
<h3>Genome sequencing and genome size estimation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Paired-end library with insert size of 220 base pairs (bp) was constructed from randomly fragmented genomic DNA, following the standard protocol (Illumina, Beijing, China). Sequence data was generated by Beijing Biomarker Technologies Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China), using an Illumina HiSeq 2500 sequencing platform. The read length was 126 bp, and clean reads were obtained after filtering and correction of the sequence data, and were relatively accurate for estimating the size of the genome, repetitive sequences, and heterozygosis. Then, based on K-mer analysis, information on peak depth and the number of 17-mers was obtained. Its relationship was expressed by using the following algorithm: Genome size = K-mer num/Peak depth [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref020" class="ref-tip">20</a>].</p>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec005" name="sec005" class="link-target" title="Sequence assembly and guanine plus cytosine (GC) content analysis"></a>
<h3>Sequence assembly and guanine plus cytosine (GC) content analysis</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>SOAPdenovo software [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref021" class="ref-tip">21</a>] and Abyss were applied for genome assembly with the pre-processed reads, where k-mer sizes of 31, 54, 63, 70, 77, and 83 were examined using default parameters, and the optimal k-mer size was selected from the N50 length. The usable reads &gt; 200 bases in length were selected to realign the contig sequences because the sequences &lt; 200 bp were likely to be derived from repetitive or low-quality sequences. Then, the paired-end relationship between reads was coincident between contigs. The scaffolds were constructed step by step using insert size paired-ends. The 10-kb non-overlapping sliding windows along the assembled sequence were used to calculate GC average sequencing depth.</p>
</div>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec006" name="sec006" class="link-target" title="Repetitive sequences"></a>
<h3>Repetitive sequences</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Due to the relatively low conservatism of the repetitive sequence among species, a particular repetitive sequence database was built to predict repeat sequences. The software programs LTR_FINDER [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref022" class="ref-tip">22</a>], MITE-Hunter [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref023" class="ref-tip">23</a>], RepeatScout [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref024" class="ref-tip">24</a>], and PILER-DF [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref025" class="ref-tip">25</a>] were used to construct a <em>de novo</em> repeat library, classified by PASTEClassifier [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref026" class="ref-tip">26</a>], and combined with the Repbase transposable element library [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref027" class="ref-tip">27</a>] to act as the final library. Then, the software RepeatMasker [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref028" class="ref-tip">28</a>] was run to find homologous repeats in the final library. SSR motifs were identified using the SciRoKo software [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref029" class="ref-tip">29</a>] in the ‘MISA’ mode, with default parameters. The minimum numbers of SSR repeats for mono-, di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, and hexa-nucleotides adopted for identification were 14, 7, 5, 4, 4, and 4, respectively.</p>
</div>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec007" name="sec007" class="link-target" title="Gene prediction and annotation"></a>
<h3>Gene prediction and annotation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>For <em>de novo</em> prediction, after filtering scaffolds of &lt; 1000 bp in size, Genscan [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref030" class="ref-tip">30</a>] and Augustus were used to predict genes with parameters trained on <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>. Then, BLAST alignment was performed between predicted genes and common databases such as Nt, Nr, TrEMBL, Swiss-Prot, Pfam, ‘euKaryotic clusters of Orthologous Groups’ (KOG), Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG), plant Gene ontology (GO), and Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COG). Meanwhile, the described genes were classified into the KOG slim categories, the GO categories, and then mapped onto the KEGG reference pathways as described by Hirakawa <em>et al</em>. [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref031" class="ref-tip">31</a>]. For homology-based prediction, protein sequences for <em>Malus×domestica</em>, <em>Pyrus bretschneideri</em>, <em>Fragaria vesca</em>, <em>Prunus mume</em>, <em>Prunus persica</em>, and <em>Vitis vinifera</em> were downloaded from publicly available databases. The putative genes of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> were clustered by using OrthoMCL [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref032" class="ref-tip">32</a>] with the unigene sets of apple, pear, strawberry, <em>Prunus mume</em>, and peach. Single-copy protein sequences of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> and the 6 other species were used to construct the evolutionary tree by using the software PHYML [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref033" class="ref-tip">33</a>].</p>
</div>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec008" name="sec008" class="link-target" title="Genes involved in AsA metabolism"></a>
<h3>Genes involved in AsA metabolism</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>AsA and DHA were measured according to the method described by An <em>et al</em>. [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref034" class="ref-tip">34</a>]. For qRT-PCR validation, 11 cDNAs encoding GDP-mannose-3',5'-epimerase (GME), GDP- L-galactose-1-phosphate phosphorylase (GGP), L-galactose-1-phosphate phosphatase (GPP), L-galactose dehydrogenase (GDH), L-galactono-1,4-lactone dehydrogenase (GLDH), D-galacturonate reductase (GUR), <em>myo</em>-inositol oxygenase (MIOX), AAO, APX, DHAR, and MDHAR proteins, all of which have potential roles in AsA metabolism, were selected. Target gene primers were designed (<a href="#pone.0147530.s003">S1 Table</a>) according to acquired sequences using the Primer Express software (Applied Biosystems, USA). Total RNAs were extracted from <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> leaves at a leaf age of 10 days while fully expanding, 50-day-old leaves were labeled as mature, and 90-day-old leaves were designated as aged, using the TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen), followed by purification with an RNA purification kit (Takara). qRT-PCR and subsequent data analysis was performed according to the method described by Yan <em>et al</em>. [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>].</p>
</div>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec009" name="sec009" data-toc="sec009" class="link-target" title="Results"></a><h2>Results</h2>
<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec010" name="sec010" class="link-target" title="Genome sequencing and genome size estimation"></a>
<h3>Genome sequencing and genome size estimation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>After the sequence data was filtering and correction, a total of 30.29 Gb clean reads were generated from the small-insert (220 bp) library, with 95.14% Q20 bases (base quality &gt; 20), about 62.99× coverage (<a href="#pone-0147530-t001">Table 1</a>), much greater than 30× coverage, which was required for successful assembly. All of the clean data were used for K-mer analysis. For the 17-mer frequency distribution (<a href="#pone-0147530-g002">Fig 2</a>), the number of K-mers was 26,445,309,972, and the peak of the depth distribution was at 54.98×. The estimated genome size was 480.97 Mb, which was calculated by using the following formula: Genome size = K-mer num/Peak depth. Similarly, a certain repeat rate could cause a repeat peak at the position of the integer multiples of the main peak, ~106×, so the genome size of repetitive sequences was estimated to be 291.49Mb, which was about 60.60% of the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> genome. In addition, the heterozygosis rate could cause a sub-peak at a position half of the height of the main peak, ~26×, which indicates about 0.18% of the heterozygosis rate in this genome.</p>

                </a></div><a href="article/figure/image?download&amp;size=original&amp;id=info:doi/10.1371/journal.pone.0147530.t003">
                original image
              </a></li></ul></div><div class="figcaption"><span>Table 3. </span> Statistics of repetitive sequence.</div><p class="caption_target"></p><p class="caption_object"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0147530.t003">
              https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0147530.t003</a></p></div><a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>90.84 Mb transposable elements (TE) were obtained, comprised 29.20% of the genome (<a href="#pone-0147530-t003">Table 3</a>), in which retroelements and DNA transoson were identified. Retroelements, also called class I transposable element (<a href="#pone-0147530-t003">Table 3</a>), comprised 24.90% of the genome. And DNA transposons, also named class II transposable element (<a href="#pone-0147530-t003">Table 3</a>), comprised only 4.30% of the genome. Long terminal repeats (LTRs) were observed to be the most abundant repeat elements, comprised 16.74% of the genome, in which 6.90% was <em>gypsy</em>, 9.76% was <em>copia</em> and other LTRs occupied only 0.08% (<a href="#pone-0147530-t003">Table 3</a>). The ratio (0.71:1) of <em>gypsy</em>-like to <em>copia</em>-like elements was calculated. There were 1.15% SSRs and 16.92% uncharacterized repeats (<a href="#pone-0147530-t003">Table 3</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>A total of 167,859 SSRs were identified and among which mono-nucleotide repeats showed predominant type, which accounted for 66.30% of the observed SSRs, followed by the di- (25.67%), tri- (6.64%), tetra- (1.08%), penta- (0.16%), and hexa- (0.15%) nucleotide repeats (<a href="#pone-0147530-t004">Table 4</a>). Mono-nucleotide repeats have been reported to be the most common type of repeats whether in monocot species, such as rice, sorghum, and <em>Brachypodium</em> or in dicot species, for example, <em>Arabidopsis</em>, <em>Medicago</em>, and <em>Populus</em>, which accounted for 79% in <em>Medicago</em> at most [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref044" class="ref-tip">44</a>]. The mono-, di- and tri-nucleotide repeats contributed to nearly 99% of SSRs in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>, and a very small portion was contributed by tetra-, penta- and hexa-nucleotide repeats. Moreover, 363 motif types were identified in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> genome, including 2 of mono-, 8 of di-, 30 of tri-, 80 of tetra-, 91 of penta-, and 152 of hexa-nucleotide repeats (<a href="#pone.0147530.s005">S3 Table</a>). Within the dinucleotide repeat motifs, the AG/CT was most abundant, which accounted for 28.81%, followed by GA/TC at 27.71% (<a href="#pone-0147530-g004">Fig 4</a>). And among the trinucleotide repeat motifs, the common motifs were GAA/TTC and ATT/AAT, accounting for 14.76% and 13.55%, respectively (<a href="#pone-0147530-g005">Fig 5</a>).</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec013" name="sec013" class="link-target" title="Gene prediction and annotation"></a>
<h3>Gene prediction and annotation</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Based on the genome of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>, with a filtering scaffold of &lt; 1,000 bp for <em>de novo</em> prediction, program Augustus got a predicted gene number of 20,589, and a total of 22,721 genes were predicted by Genescan (<a href="#pone-0147530-t005">Table 5</a>). We choose Genescan for further analyses. The identified genes have an average length of 2,311.52 bp, an average exon length of 228.15 bp, and intron length of 401.18 bp. The number of predicted genes in the genome of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was much lower than that of other sequenced genomes such as <em>Malus×domestica</em> (57,386) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>], <em>Pyrus bretschneideri</em> (42,812) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>], <em>Fragaria vesca</em> (34,809) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>], <em>Prunus mume</em> (31,390) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref016" class="ref-tip">16</a>], and <em>Prunus persica</em> (27,852) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>]. It has been reported that the insufficient sequence depth coverage, variable regulation of gene expression levels, and low sequence homology because of limited gene information from closely related species might be possible reasons [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref045" class="ref-tip">45</a>].</p>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec014" name="sec014" class="link-target" title="Putative genes associated with AsA metabolism"></a>
<h3>Putative genes associated with AsA metabolism</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Based on the genome survey sequencing dataset, 17 unique sequences were annotated as paralogs of 11 genes associated with AsA metabolism. Of these 17 sequences, two (Roxburghii008246-TA and Roxburghii018888-TA), three (Roxburghii002764-TA, Roxburghii-016536-TA, and Roxburghii016678-TA), and four (Roxburghii008076-TA, Roxburghii015197-TA, Roxburghii016587-TA, and Roxburghii018922-TA) were annotated as paralogs of <em>MDHAR</em>, <em>MIOX</em>, and <em>APX</em>, respectively, and the other 8 were in one-to-one correspondence (<a href="#pone.0147530.s007">S5 Table</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>To confirm experimentally that the genes obtained from sequencing were actually expressed, all of the 11 putative genes involved in AsA biosynthesis, namely, <em>GME</em> (Roxburghii013562-TA), <em>GGP</em> (Roxburghii021760-TA), <em>GPP</em> (Roxburghii007418-TA), <em>GDH</em> (Roxburghii012479-TA), <em>GLDH</em> (Roxburghii012337-TA), <em>MIOX</em> (Roxburghii002764-TA), <em>GUR</em> (Roxburghii012921-TA); in AsA oxidation, namely, <em>AAO</em> (Roxburghii002218-TA) and <em>APX</em> (Roxburghii008076-TA); and AsA recycling, including <em>DHAR</em> (Roxburghii013431-TA) and <em>MDHAR</em> (Roxburghii-008246-TA), were analyzed by qRT-PCR across three leaf developmental ages.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p3" class="link-target"></a><p><a href="#pone-0147530-g008">Fig 8</a> shows that all selected genes were expressed at varying levels during the three developmental stages, in which the expression of three genes involved in AsA synthesis, namely, <em>GLDH</em>, <em>GUR</em>, and <em>MIOX</em>, and two genes involved in AsA degradation, <em>AAO</em> and <em>APX</em>, reached highest abundance in mature leaves, and then markedly decreased until these aged. Similarly, leaf DHA and T-AsA (AsA + DHA) levels increased with leaf development, reaching its peak levels in mature leaves and then rapidly decreased. These results suggest that the AsA pool size in <em>Rosa roxburghii</em> leaves were regulated by biosynthesis, as well as recycling.</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="sec015" name="sec015" data-toc="sec015" class="link-target" title="Discussion"></a><h2>Discussion</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Flow cytometry has been regarded as a standard method for the prediction of the genome size of plants [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref046" class="ref-tip">46</a>]. However, in the recent years, the development of the NGS technology has provided researchers an affordable means of addressing a wide range of questions relating to emerging and non-model species. In addition, the k-mer method has been successfully applied for the estimation of genome size using NGS reads without prior knowledge of the genome size. Such approach has been utilized in the analysis of the genomes of <em>Gracilariopsis lemaneiformis</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref045" class="ref-tip">45</a>], <em>Cucumis sativus</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref047" class="ref-tip">47</a>], and <em>Myrica rubra</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref048" class="ref-tip">48</a>]. The genome size estimated by K-mer depth distribution of sequenced reads is generally consistent with that of flow cytometry [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref047" class="ref-tip">47</a>]. In the present study, the estimated genome size of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was 480.97 Mb, which was close to the results estimated by flow cytometry (464.55 Mb) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref049" class="ref-tip">49</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref050" class="ref-tip">50</a>].</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p2" name="article1.body1.sec4.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>Fruit trees are perennial, and the majority of these are highly heterozygous; therefore, the assembly of fruit tree genomes is relatively difficult using the WGS strategy. Homozygous materials for genome sequencing were always in priority [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref045" class="ref-tip">45</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref051" class="ref-tip">51</a>], although a complicated bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) approach could resolve problems associated with the assembly of a heterozygous genome [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>]. Based on the feasibility of estimating heterozygosity from low-coverage genome sequence [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref052" class="ref-tip">52</a>], a heterozygosity rate of 0.18% was observed in the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>, which was higher than that of other sequenced plants such as pigeon pea (0.067%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref053" class="ref-tip">53</a>], <em>Prunus mume</em> (0.08%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref016" class="ref-tip">16</a>], but much lower than that of black cottonwood (0.26%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref054" class="ref-tip">54</a>] and date palm (0.46%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref039" class="ref-tip">39</a>], which could be utilized in genome studies using the WGS strategy.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p3" name="article1.body1.sec4.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>Several investigations have determined a genome size range of 294–782 Mb for at least 33 rose species and several cultivars [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref055" class="ref-tip">55</a>]. This observed variability in genome size is not likely due to differences in gene numbers but rather to variations in non-coding sequences such as the intron size [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref056" class="ref-tip">56</a>], and a variety of other factors, including the copy number of TEs, the amount or size of SSRs, the size of inter-enhancer spacers, and the number of pseudogenes [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref057" class="ref-tip">57</a>]. For example, the observed genome size difference between apple and pear is mainly due to repetitive sequences that are predominantly contributed by TEs, whereas the size of the genic regions is similar in both species [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>]. In addition, different TE compositions, especially the composition of LTRs, resulting from TE multiplication, may cause genome size changes, which might have large effects on speciation [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref058" class="ref-tip">58</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref059" class="ref-tip">59</a>]. In the present study, The ratio (0.71 to 1) of <em>gypsy</em> to <em>copia</em> LTRs in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was remarkably lower than that observed in peach (1.16 to 1) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>], strawberry (1.20 to 1) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>], pear (1.99 to 1) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>], and apple (4.58 to 1) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>]. These results could contribute to the understanding of Rosaceae genome evolution [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref060" class="ref-tip">60</a>].</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p4" name="article1.body1.sec4.p4" class="link-target"></a><p>The TE content in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was 29.20%, which was similar to that of peach (29.60%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref017" class="ref-tip">17</a>]. In addition, the amount of LTRs, which comprised 16.74% of the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> genome, was similar to that of strawberry (~16%) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref015" class="ref-tip">15</a>]. However, the genome size of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> was ~2-fold larger than that of the two species. This difference in genome size might not be due to the amount of TEs or LTRs, but the composition of LTRs. Meanwhile, SSRs comprised 1.15% of the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> genome, which was significantly larger than that observed in apple (0.27) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref014" class="ref-tip">14</a>], <em>Pyrus bretschneideri</em> (0.22) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref018" class="ref-tip">18</a>], and <em>Pyrus communis</em> (0.04) [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref019" class="ref-tip">19</a>], and might have potentially led to the genome expansion of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p5" name="article1.body1.sec4.p5" class="link-target"></a><p>Genomic SSR markers, reliable, highly polymorphic, often multi-allelic, and easy to amplify, are widely used in genetic diversity, genetic map construction and so on [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref053" class="ref-tip">53</a>]. However, the lack of available genomic resources in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> impeded the use of microsatellite markers. To date, the limited EST-SSR markers were developed for <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>], but no genome-wide SSR markers have been published. Presently, the genome survey based on NGS is an especially useful method to explore SSR markers for tree crops [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref061" class="ref-tip">61</a>].</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p6" name="article1.body1.sec4.p6" class="link-target"></a><p>Compared to fruits [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>, <a href="#pone.0147530.ref034" class="ref-tip">34</a>], <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> leaves undergo a higher level of active oxidation loss and recycling of AsA. The L-galactose pathway is considered as the dominant route for AsA biosynthesis in several plant species [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref062" class="ref-tip">62</a>], and <em>GGP</em> may play a key role in the L-galactose pathway in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> fruits [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref009" class="ref-tip">9</a>]. In the present study, <em>GGP</em> was not highly expressed in AsA-abundant aged leaves, although the level of <em>GLDH</em> expression was similar to the variable pattern of T-AsA content. Besides, the discovery of <em>GUR</em> and <em>MIOX</em> genes in the present study suggests that <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> can use GalUA or <em>myo-</em>inositol as an initial substrate in AsA biosynthesis, implying that multiple pathways were involved in AsA metabolism in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> leaves. In addition, <em>GUR</em> via the GalUA pathway played important roles in AsA biosynthesis in strawberry fruits [<a href="#pone.0147530.ref063" class="ref-tip">63</a>]. <em>APX</em>, which encodes a well-recognized enzyme, catalyzes the oxidation of AsA with high specificity, and <em>AAO</em>, which encodes another vital redox enzyme, also catalyzes the oxidation of apoplast AsA in the presence of oxygen. These two upregulated genes might have caused DHA accumulation in mature leaves (<a href="#pone-0147530-g008">Fig 8</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec4.p7" name="article1.body1.sec4.p7" class="link-target"></a><p>This is the first report of genome-wide characterization in the genus <em>Rosa</em>. Among the 100–250 species in this genus, <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> is most important in terms of its horticultural, nutritional, and medicinal value. However, its limited genomic information has constrained genetic studies of <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>. A total of 167,859 SSRs and 22,721 genes derived from the <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em> genome survey could help in the construction of high-density linkage maps and in conducting gene-based association studies. In addition, the generated dataset could contribute to the understanding of Rosaceae genome evolution. Evaluation of the expression of candidate genes involved in AsA metabolism may improve our understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying ascorbate accumulation in <em>R</em>. <em>roxburghii</em>.</p>
