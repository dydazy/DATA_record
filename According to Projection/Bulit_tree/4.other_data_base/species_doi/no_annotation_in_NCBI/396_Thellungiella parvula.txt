This species is endemic to highly saline environments subject to extreme temperatures. The genome was primarily assembled using next-generation sequencing data.                 <section aria-labelledby="Abs2" data-title="Abstract" lang="en"><div class="c-article-section" id="Abs2-section"><h2 class="c-article-section__title js-section-title js-c-reading-companion-sections-item" id="Abs2">Abstract</h2><div class="c-article-section__content" id="Abs2-content"><p><i>Thellungiella parvula</i><sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 1" title="Al-Shehbaz, I.A. &amp; O'Kane, S.L. Placement of Arabidopsis parvula in Thellungiella (Brassicaceae). Novon. 5, 309–310 (1995)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR1" id="ref-link-section-d38933e546">1</a></sup> is related to <i>Arabidopsis thaliana</i> and is endemic to saline, resource-poor habitats<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 2" title="Amtmann, A. Learning from evolution: Thellungiella generates new knowledge on essential and critical components of abiotic stress tolerance in plants. Mol. Plant 2, 3–12 (2009)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR2" id="ref-link-section-d38933e553">2</a></sup>, making it a model for the evolution of plant adaptation to extreme environments. Here we present the draft genome for this extremophile species. Exclusively by next generation sequencing, we obtained the <i>de novo</i> assembled genome in 1,496 gap-free contigs, closely approximating the estimated genome size of 140 Mb. We anchored these contigs to seven pseudo chromosomes without the use of maps. We show that short reads can be assembled to a near-complete chromosome level for a eukaryotic species lacking prior genetic information. The sequence identifies a number of tandem duplications that, by the nature of the duplicated genes, suggest a possible basis for <i>T. parvula</i>'s extremophile lifestyle. Our results provide essential background for developing genomically influenced testable hypotheses for the evolution of environmental stress tolerance.</p></div></div></section>

                    <section aria-labelledby="Sec1" data-title="Main"><div class="c-article-section" id="Sec1-section"><h2 class="c-article-section__title js-section-title js-c-reading-companion-sections-item" id="Sec1">Main</h2><div class="c-article-section__content" id="Sec1-content"><p>According to phylogenetic studies based on fossil evidence<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 3" title="Beilstein, M.A. et al. Dated molecular phylogenies indicate a Miocene origin for Arabidopsis thaliana. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 107, 18724–18728 (2010)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR3" id="ref-link-section-d38933e594">3</a></sup>, the spilt between <i>A. thaliana</i> and the main Brassica group encompassing <i>T. parvula</i> in the subclade <i>Eutremeae</i> is thought to have occurred about 43 million years ago. Both <i>T. parvula</i> and <i>A. thaliana</i> have similar genome sizes, and their close taxonomic relationship provides unique opportunities for tracing evolutionary rearrangements between the two species.</p><p>The main goal of this project was to produce a <i>de novo</i>, scaffold-level, gap-free assembly of the <i>T. parvula</i> genome. To achieve this, we used second generation sequencing exclusively, including ROCHE-454 GS FLX Titanium sequencing for its read length advantage and Illumina GA2 sequencing for its higher quality reads. We included varying insert sizes of paired-end libraries in addition to single-end reads (Online Methods). In total, we obtained 7.8 × 10<sup>9</sup> high quality basepairs, equivalent to <span class="stix">∼</span>50-fold genome coverage. Of these, 85% came from the 454 sequencing (<a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="supplementary material anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#MOESM6">Supplementary Fig. 1</a> and <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="supplementary material anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#MOESM6">Supplementary Table 1</a>).</p><p>In the absence of genetic maps, with only limited physiological studies<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 4" title="Orsini, F. et al. A comparative study of salt tolerance parameters in 11 wild relatives of Arabidopsis thaliana. J. Exp. Bot. 61, 3787–3798 (2010)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR4" id="ref-link-section-d38933e634">4</a></sup>, without prior genome information and with only very limited transcriptome sequences, we used an iterative hybrid approach to construct a draft genome (Online Methods). The result was a total of 1,496 meta-contigs (scaffolds) of merged primary contigs, ranging in size from 1 kb to 13.08 Mb (<a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="table anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Tab1">Table 1</a>). However, unlike typical scaffold sequences, these meta-contigs were free of gaps. Overall, 73% of the length of the <i>T. parvula</i> draft genome was represented in 20 contigs longer than 1.5 Mb, and 85% of the sequenced genome was represented by the largest 60 contigs, each 100 kb or greater in length. Based on flow cytometry of propidium-iodide–stained nuclei<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 5" title="Oh, D.-H. et al. Extremophiles' presence in evolutionarily distinct lineages reveals genetic complexities that appear to have evolved from the common genetic makeup of all plants. In adaptation to various combinations of environmental stresses, these extremophiles show tolerance of stresses against which crop plants in particular have no defenses. Knowing how extremophiles operate can, however, instruct us about the underlying genetic requisites and mechanisms for successful stress defenses. In this report, we have now shown that it is possible to determine the genome sequence of extremophiles, as well as model glycophytes, exclusively relying on next-generation DNA sequencing tools and <i>de novo</i> assembly.</p><p>The availability of the <i>T. parvula</i> genome provides a unique view of chromosome structure, organization and gene complement. Of particular importance is the comparison of this genome with that of the related <i>A. thaliana</i>, which is unquestionably a stress-sensitive species. In our initial analysis, this halophyte, with a genome only <span class="stix">∼</span>15% larger than that of <i>A. thaliana</i>, shows striking differences in gene complement. The differences are partly because of tandem duplications in <i>T. parvula</i> of single copy genes in <i>A. thaliana</i> and preferential amplification of genes with known or assumed functions in stress defense responses. Within these differences, we expect, lie the unique solutions to understanding <i>T. parvula</i>'s particular lifestyle and adaptation to its demanding ecological niche. More detailed examination of genome structure, coding complexity, and gene structure and expression in stress response pathways in comparative studies will point the way toward correlating the <i>T. parvula</i> phenotype with its genetic makeup.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec3">URLs.</h3><p>SeqAnswers online forum, <a href="http://seqanswers.com/">http://seqanswers.com/</a>; The seeds were derived from a single plant propagated from single seeds over eight successive generations. The original accession was collected from a salt lake in Tuz Golu, central Turkey at an elevation of 905 m above sea level. At the collection site, the soil bulk density was 1.225 g/cm<sup>3</sup> with 32.4% salts by weight. Genomic DNA was prepared using the Nucleon Phytopure Genomic DNA Extraction kit (GE Healthcare).</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec6">Strategy for a highly contiguous draft genome.</h3><p>Compared to Sanger sequencing, the shorter reads associated with either 454 or Illumina sequencing manifest decreased connectivity. As a result, considerably deeper coverage is required to generate contiguous assemblies. Deeper coverage alone, however, does not in itself solve the problem of fragmented assemblies; if reads are shorter than a repeat, gaps are unavoidable, and with deeper coverage, accumulated sequencing errors make assembly more computationally challenging. In assembling the <i>T. parvula</i> genome, the problem was mitigated by (i) using reads from different technologies, (ii) using paired reads with different insert lengths to span different repeat lengths and (iii) computationally selecting high quality reads.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec7">Overview of sequencing, assembly and annotation.</h3><p>Library construction and sequencing were performed in the W.M. Keck Center for Comparative and Functional Genomics at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. Random shotgun genomic libraries were constructed according to the manufacturer's recommendations for each of the two pyrosequencing platforms, GS FLX Titanium (454 Life Sciences) and Illumina GA2 (Illumina). Newbler (454-Roche), ABySS<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 29" title="Simpson, J.T. et al. ABySS: a parallel assembler for short read sequence data. Genome Res. 19, 1117–1123 (2009)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR29" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2718">29</a></sup> and minimus2 (ref. <a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 30" title="Sommer, D.D. et al. Minimus: a fast, lightweight genome assembler. BMC Bioinformatics 8, 64 (2007)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR30" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2721">30</a>) were used as the main assembly programs to generate the draft genome, and FGENESH++ (SoftBerry), GENSCAN, BLAST (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>) and Blast2GO<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 12" title="Conesa, A. &amp; Götz, S. Plant Genomics 2008, 619832 (2008)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR12" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2728">12</a></sup> were used to predict and annotate gene models.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec8">DNA library preparation and sequencing.</h3><p>For 454 pyrosequencing, both shotgun and paired-end libraries were constructed. Genomic DNA was randomly sheared by nebulization to fragments of 500–800 bp in length to construct two shotgun libraries. Additional DNA was processed to construct paired-end libraries with size spans of 3 kb (three libraries), 8 kb (two libraries) and 20 kb (two libraries). All libraries were constructed, clonally amplified and sequenced on the 454 Genome Sequencer FLX-Titanium according to the manufacturer's kits and protocols (454 Life Sciences). Signal processing and base calling were performed using the bundled GS FLX software version 2.0.01.</p><p>For Illumina sequencing, genomic DNA was nebulized, and fragments 200–500 bp in length were size selected to construct a shotgun library using the Illumina Genomic DNA Sample Prep kit (Illumina). The library was sequenced on three lanes of a flowcell from one end (single read) for 81 cycles on a Genome Analyzer IIx. The Illumina Pipeline 1.5 was used to generate fastq sequence files from the raw data.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec9">Hybrid genome assembly.</h3><p>A combined total of 7.8 × 10<sup>9</sup> bases resulted from sequencing using both platforms. Average read sizes were 355 bp and 80 bp for the 454 and Illumina sequences, respectively. Approximately 85% of all sequences were derived from the 454 sequencing. We followed an iterative approach for assembly starting from raw sequence reads assembled into primary contigs. We used two assembly programs and combined the primary contigs and paired-end data to build scaffolds in successive assemblies. Single and paired-end 454 sequences were assembled using the Roche GS assembler, Newbler (version 2.0.01.14), with a 40 bp minimum overlap and 90% identity. In both instances, reads were first assembled as single-end reads, after which the paired-end information was used to construct scaffolds.</p><p>To assemble Illumina reads, we tested both Velvet<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 31" title="Zerbino, D.R. &amp; Birney, E. Velvet: Algorithms for de novo short read assembly using de Bruijn graphs. Genome Res. 18, 821–829 (2008)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR31" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2757">31</a></sup> (v1.3) and ABySS<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 29" title="Simpson, J.T. et al. ABySS: a parallel assembler for short read sequence data. Genome Res. 19, 1117–1123 (2009)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR29" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2761">29</a></sup> (v1.2) short-read assemblers using only reads that passed the Illumina chastity filter (base call values for chastity greater than 0.6 in the first 25 cycles); the k-mer size was set to 31 bp, and the coverage cutoff was set to 4. Both assemblers produced comparable results, but ABySS was much faster and was, therefore, chosen for further optimized short read assemblies. We also used Newbler contigs as single reads with the Illumina reads as the input in ABySS. We tested every odd-numbered length from 29 bp to 61 bp as the k-mer size to find the optimal size, meaning that which yielded the longest N50 and the fewest total contigs while maintaining total contig length near the flow-cytrometry–estimated genome size of 160–180 Mb.</p><p>Because ABySS can be very sensitive to sequencing errors such as short indels, when using raw 454 reads in ABySS, custom Perl scripts were used to remove any raw 454 read that had homopolymers exceeding 10 bases (all 454 homopolymer error reads cannot be removed but can be minimized). To enable the scaffold generating step in ABySS to proceed when 454 raw paired-ends reads were used, the program sff_extract (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>) was used to process the standard flowgram (sff) files generated by the GS FLX sequencer. Different k-mer sizes were selected based on the different paired-end libraries used for scaffolding, but in most instances, the optimum k-mer size found with ABySS was 41.</p><p>The collection of contigs and scaffolds created in the primary assemblies was an overlapping set with a high level of redundancy. To select a non-redundant set, we used mkvtree in the Vmatch suite (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>) to index the sequences by length; Vmatch was used to cluster sequences, including clusters with size of 1 (singlets). We matched for 100% identity and full coverage of the smaller sequences in pairs. Contigs longer than 1,000 bp were used for further processing. This set was further inspected with all-against-all BLAST searches and the aligner NUCmer in MUMmer 3.22 (ref. <a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 32" title="Kurtz, S. et al. Versatile and open software for comparing large genomes. Genome Biol. 5, R12 (2004)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR32" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2776">32</a>) to remove duplicate contigs that may have been assembled for the same region of the genome.</p><p>The meta-assembly of selected contigs from primary assemblies was carried out with the overlap-layout-consensus assembler, Minimus2 in the AMOS pipeline (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>), using a minimum 40-bp overlap with 95% identity. The resulting contigs and singlets were combined and purged of further redundancy, contaminating DNA and mitochondrial and chloroplast DNA using BLAST searches. This resulted in 1,496 contigs with a total length of <span class="stix">∼</span>137 Mb.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec10">Genome annotation.</h3><p>The <i>T. parvula</i> draft genome was masked for repetitive sequences by RepeatMasker<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 33" title="Jurka, J. et al. Repbase Update, a database of eukaryotic repetitive elements. Cytogenet. Genome Res. 110, 462–467 (2005)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR33" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2798">33</a></sup> searching Repbase 14.01 and with BLASTn using the Plant Repeat database (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>). The masked contigs for known repetitive elements were further analyzed with NUCmer and custom scripts to search for long tandem repeats and for <i>T. parvula</i>–specific unclassified, non-exact, long repeats. Any sequences that were found more than five times were considered as repeats in this search.</p><p>FGENESH++ (SoftBerry) was used to predict protein coding ORFs in the <i>T. parvula</i> draft genome masked for repetitive sequences, with parameters optimized for dicot plants and protein sequences from the NCBI non-redundant (NR) database as reference. A total of 29,338 ORFs were predicted, of which 437 were further annotated as transposable elements based on BLASTn searches. Genomic regions that contained FGENESH++-predicted ORFs with lengths similar to their <i>Arabidopsis</i> homologs (± 20%) were tested with another gene prediction program, GENESCAN. When the predictions from the two programs deviated for the same genomic region, the ORF closest in length to another known homologous cDNA was taken as the more likely prediction. All genomic contigs and predicted ORFs were searched against NCBI nucleotide and protein databases and TAIR9 cDNA database (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>) using BLASTn and BLASTx searches. The predicted proteins were further annotated with the Blast2GO pipeline<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 12" title="Conesa, A. &amp; Götz, S. Blast2GO: A comprehensive suite for functional analysis in plant genomics. Intl. J. Plant Genomics 2008, 619832 (2008)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR12" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2820">12</a></sup> to assign GO and GOslim-plant terms based on NCBI plant databases and InterProScan<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 34" title="Zdobnov, E.M. &amp; Apweiler, R. InterProScan—an integration platform for the signature-recognition methods in InterPro. Bioinformatics 17, 847–848 (2001)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR34" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2824">34</a></sup>. To obtain experimental evidence for our <i>ab initio</i> predictions, we mapped the ORFs to high quality Illumina reads trimmed to 80 bp from a transcriptome sequence library generated from young seedlings. Using the program Bowtie<sup><a data-track="click" data-track-action="reference anchor" data-track-label="link" data-test="citation-ref" aria-label="Reference 35" title="Langmead, B., Trapnell, C., Pop, M. &amp; Salzberg, S.L. Ultrafast and memory-efficient alignment of short DNA sequences to the human genome. Genome Biol. 10, R25 (2009)." href="/articles/ng.889#ref-CR35" id="ref-link-section-d38933e2832">35</a></sup> with 100% identity to a minimum length of 50 bp and with '-m' set to 1 to ensure unique mapping, we found that 73% of the high quality reads mapped uniquely to the predicted ORFs (<a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="supplementary material anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#MOESM8">Supplementary Table 4</a>). The remaining reads are too repetitive in nature, map to multiple ORFs or contain low complexity regions and are therefore unusable in mapping.</p><p>BLAST searches were performed to identify miRNA genes and other RNA genes by searching against the miRBase database of plant miRNA collections (release 16) and the Rfam database (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>) (release 10) for other non-coding RNA families including rRNA and tRNA genes.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec11">Statistical analyses.</h3><p>When comparing the distributions of GO subcategories between <i>A. thaliana</i> and <i>T. parvula</i> (<a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="figure anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Fig2">Figs. 2</a> and <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="figure anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Fig3">3</a>), two-tailed <i>χ</i><sup>2</sup> tests were used (see <a data-track="click" data-track-label="link" data-track-action="section anchor" href="/articles/ng.889#Sec3">URLs</a>). For each GO subcategory, a 2 × 2 contingency table was constructed by recording the numbers of genes included or not included in a subcategory for each species and ranking the statistical significance of the differences.</p><h3 class="c-article__sub-heading" id="Sec12">Accession codes.</h3><p>The raw reads for this project are deposited in the NCBI SRA project under the accession number <a href="http://trace.ddbj.nig.ac.jp/DRASearch/submission?acc=SRA026763">SRA026763</a>. The Illumina reads can be accessed under <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra?term=SRX047632">SRX047632</a> and the 454 reads under <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra?term=SRX032604">SRX032604</a>. The genome assembly is deposited with the NCBI Genome Project ID <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/?term=63843">63843</a>, and the sequences are deposited with the GenBank ID <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/?term=AFAN00000000.1">AFAN00000000.1</a>.</p></div></div></section>
